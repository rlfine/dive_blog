Gas Theory and Scuba Diving

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

When we descend beneath the waves, we are not just exploring the underwater world — we are entering a realm where gas physics governs every moment of the dive...

What Is Gas Theory?

Gas theory refers to the scientific study of how gases behave under different pressure, temperature, and volume conditions.

Several gas theory laws are specifically applicable to scuba diving. In this setting, we will unpack each and apply the theory to our dive planning process. Along the way, hopefully, we will see the laws of gasses not as a pedagogical exercise needed to score a passing mark on an e-learning course for a needed certification card. Instead, hopefully, we will see the gas laws as important tools that we have available to assist us in our dive.

Let's get started.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures and its Importance in Diving

Dalton’s Law states that the total pressure exerted by a mixture of gases equals the sum of the pressures of each individual gas. Each gas in a mixture behaves independently, and its contribution to the total pressure is proportional to its concentration. In diving, this principle becomes crucial because the deeper a diver descends, the higher the absolute pressure and, therefore, the higher the partial pressure of each gas the diver breathes.

One of the most critical applications of Dalton’s Law for divers is monitoring the partial pressure of oxygen (PPO2). Oxygen toxicity becomes a significant risk when the PPO2 exceeds 1.4 ata during the working portion of a dive. Therefore, divers must understand how to calculate their maximum operating depth (MOD) based on the oxygen content of their breathing gas.

Maximum Operating Depth (MOD) formula:
MOD = [(PPO2 Limit) ÷ (Fraction of O2)] - 1 ATA

Example for EANx32:
MOD = [(1.4 ÷ 0.32) - 1] × 33 = 111 feet (saltwater)

For example, when diving with EANx32 (32% oxygen), the MOD for a PPO2 limit of 1.4 ata in seawater is approximately 111 feet. For EANx36, the MOD becomes shallower, around 95 feet. This calculation is vital because exceeding the PPO2 limit can lead to central nervous system (CNS) oxygen toxicity, potentially causing convulsions underwater.

It’s a common misconception that diving with Nitrox extends a diver’s maximum depth. It does not. In fact, Nitrox generally reduces the allowable depth compared to air. The true advantage of Nitrox (EANx) is that it reduces the percentage of nitrogen a diver absorbs per breath, which extends no-decompression limits (NDLs). However, Nitrox does not eliminate nitrogen narcosis. The narcosis effect is primarily related to depth and pressure rather than the specific mix of nitrogen and oxygen.

Nitrogen Narcosis Quick Facts

When does it start?
Most divers experience the effects of nitrogen narcosis when the partial pressure of nitrogen (PpN₂) reaches between 3.2 and 3.5 ATA.

What depth does that occur?

  • Air (79% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin between 100 and 120 feet (30–36 meters).
  • EANx32 (68% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin slightly deeper, around 120 to 135 feet (36–41 meters).
  • EANx36 (64% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin around 125 to 140 feet (38–43 meters).

Although Nitrox (EANx32/EANx36) reduces the percentage of nitrogen and may slightly delay the onset of narcosis, the practical depth limits for Nitrox divers are much shallower due to oxygen toxicity concerns. For example, the maximum operating depth (MOD) of EANx36 at a PPO2 limit of 1.4 ATA is approximately 96 feet — far shallower than the depths where narcosis typically becomes a risk. Thus, divers using Nitrox are generally limited by oxygen exposure long before nitrogen narcosis becomes a significant concern.

Does Nitrox prevent narcosis?
No. Nitrox does not eliminate narcosis. While it may slightly reduce risk at moderate depths, divers must remain aware that narcosis can still occur, and other factors like depth, total pressure, and environmental stressors play a significant role.

What factors make it worse?
Cold, stress, fatigue, carbon dioxide buildup, alcohol consumption (even from the night before), and anxiety can all lower a diver’s tolerance to narcosis.

Competent divers should be able to compute their MOD based solely on their PPO2 limit and the oxygen content of their breathing gas. Mastery of this fundamental concept is a cornerstone of safe Nitrox diving and is essential for planning any dive that involves enriched air mixtures.

Boyle’s Law and Its Application in Diving

Boyle’s Law describes the relationship between pressure and volume for a gas at constant temperature: as pressure increases, volume decreases proportionally, and vice versa.

For divers, the basic expression — P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ — becomes easy to memorize when studying for a certification test. However, true mastery of diving physics requires understanding how Boyle’s Law affects real-world dive operations and buoyancy control.

Boyle's Law directly influences buoyancy during a dive. As a diver descends, external pressure increases, causing the gas in the buoyancy compensator (BC) bladder and the wetsuit’s neoprene material to compress. The diver becomes less buoyant and must add gas to the BC to compensate. During ascent, the opposite occurs: gas expands, increasing buoyancy. If uncontrolled, this expansion can accelerate ascent rates dangerously.

Boyle's Law also plays a critical role during the recovery of objects underwater. When using a lift bag to raise a submerged object, divers must understand that the air inside it expands rapidly as the bag rises. This expansion displaces more water, significantly increasing the lift force. If not managed carefully, the object can shoot upward with increasing speed, posing a serious risk to any diver positioned above or below it. Worse, if the bag breaches the surface and flips over, it can lose its air, causing the object to freefall uncontrollably back to the bottom — endangering anyone nearby.

The better diver is not merely someone who memorizes Boyle’s Law for a test but one who internalizes the principle and can apply it confidently in practical scenarios.

Mastery means anticipating how gas volume and buoyancy will change with depth, adjusting for it proactively, and making sound decisions that protect both the diver and the dive team during operations such as lift bag recoveries, deep dives, or safety stops.

Henry’s Law

Henry’s Law states that the amount of a gas that dissolves into a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid. In diving, this principle governs how inert gases like nitrogen are absorbed into — and released from — body tissues during a dive.

We introduced the idea of partial pressures earlier when discussing Dalton’s Law. Here, Henry’s Law explains what happens to those gases physiologically. As a diver descends and ambient pressure increases, the partial pressure of nitrogen in the breathing gas rises. This creates a higher gradient pressure between the lungs and the tissues, causing nitrogen to dissolve into the blood and tissues at an increased rate. During ascent, as external pressure decreases, the gradient reverses, and nitrogen begins to come out of solution. If this off-gassing occurs too quickly, it can result in the formation of bubbles — the underlying cause of decompression sickness (DCS).

The exact amount of gas absorbed into tissues depends on both the percentage of a specific gas in the breathing mixture and the differential pressure between the inspired gas and the tissues. This is why using Nitrox, such as EANx36 (36% oxygen, 64% nitrogen), can reduce nitrogen absorption compared to diving with air at the same depth. For example, at 85 feet, the partial pressure of nitrogen when breathing EANx36 is significantly lower than when breathing air, leading to reduced nitrogen loading and potentially extending no-decompression limits.

However, the tradeoff is important: while nitrogen exposure is reduced, the diver is exposed to a higher partial pressure of oxygen, which increases the risk of central nervous system (CNS) oxygen toxicity. Henry’s Law provides the foundation to quantify this tradeoff — allowing divers to plan dives that strategically mitigate some DCS risk through reduced nitrogen exposure, while carefully managing the increased oxygen exposure through maximum operating depth (MOD) limits and dive time restrictions.

Henry’s Law in Dive Planning

Lower Nitrogen Absorption:
Using enriched air (e.g., EANx32 or EANx36) reduces the partial pressure of nitrogen compared to air at the same depth, decreasing nitrogen uptake and potentially extending no-decompression limits (NDLs).

Higher Oxygen Absorption:
Enriched air increases the partial pressure of oxygen, raising the risk of central nervous system (CNS) oxygen toxicity. Divers must plan dives carefully to stay within safe PPO2 limits.

Key Tradeoff:
Nitrox diving can help mitigate decompression sickness risk by lowering nitrogen loading, but it introduces a stricter depth limit based on oxygen toxicity risks.

Understanding Henry’s Law moves a diver beyond memorizing test answers toward true mastery of dive theory. It enables divers to make informed decisions about gas mixtures, no-decompression planning, and the risks and benefits associated with enriched air diving at various depths.

Charles’ Law, Amontons’ Law, and Temperature Effects on Diving

Charles’ Law and Amontons’ Law (also called Gay-Lussac’s Law) describe how gases respond to temperature changes. Charles’ Law states that the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (in Kelvin) if pressure remains constant. Amontons’ Law states that the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature if the volume remains constant. Together, these laws explain many common experiences divers have with tanks and gas pressures in different temperature environments.

When a scuba cylinder is filled at a dive shop, the rapid compression of gas into the tank causes the temperature of the air to rise significantly. According to Amontons’ Law, this heating causes the pressure inside the tank to spike. However, as the tank cools to ambient temperature, the gas pressure drops. Unless the shop either slightly overfills while hot, or allows the tank to cool and tops it off, the diver may leave with significantly less than the intended working pressure — for example, 2800 psi instead of 3000 psi.

Divers also experience temperature-driven pressure changes during dives, particularly when encountering a thermocline. For example, jumping into a lake on a 95°F (35°C) day and descending into water near 66°F (19°C) can cause a noticeable pressure drop in the tank. A diver planning a turn pressure based on half the initial pressure (for example, between 3000 and 750 psi) may reach that turn pressure earlier than expected. As the diver ascends back into warmer water near the surface, the pressure can rise slightly, partially recovering the earlier loss.

Real-World Effects of Temperature and Pressure Changes

Tank Cooling After Filling:
When tanks are filled rapidly, they heat up, causing a temporary pressure increase. As the tank cools, pressure drops — sometimes by 200 psi or more — unless topped off after cooling.

Thermocline Pressure Drop:
Descending into colder water causes the gas inside the tank to contract slightly, lowering the indicated pressure. Divers may encounter an earlier-than-expected turn pressure if not accounting for temperature effects.

Surface Pressure Recovery:
Ascending into warmer water at the end of the dive can cause a modest increase in tank pressure as the gas warms and expands, partially offsetting earlier losses experienced at depth.

While the pressure changes from temperature shifts are relatively small compared to gas consumption, they are very real and must be accounted for. Awareness of these gas laws helps divers better predict their gas usage and avoid confusion during their dives. Charles' Law and Amontons’ Law together provide the foundation to understand these everyday diving experiences, reinforcing the importance of real gas law mastery over simple memorization.

The General Gas Law: Tying It All Together

While individual gas laws like Boyle’s, Charles’, and Amontons’ are often taught separately, in reality, they are all interconnected. The General Gas Law, also known as the Combined Gas Law, merges pressure, volume, and temperature into a single relationship: (P₁ × V₁) / T₁ = (P₂ × V₂) / T₂. This equation shows that any change in pressure, volume, or temperature must be accompanied by corresponding changes in the other variables.

For divers, this relationship explains everything from buoyancy changes to gas consumption rates and tank pressure fluctuations. Boyle’s Law describes how gas volume changes with depth; Charles’ and Amontons’ Laws explain how temperature shifts affect volume and pressure. Henry’s Law adds the critical dimension of gas absorption into body tissues based on partial pressure. Together, they form the physical framework behind virtually every diving phenomenon.

Consider a tank filled to 3000 psi at 90°F. As it cools to 70°F, the General Gas Law predicts a measurable pressure drop, even though the gas volume remains the same. Or think about a diver ascending with a lift bag: the gas inside expands because of decreasing pressure (Boyle’s Law), and if the water temperature changes along the ascent, Charles’ and Amontons’ Laws further affect the bag’s expansion rate.

Practical Implications of the General Gas Law for Divers

Buoyancy Changes with Depth and Temperature:
Gas volume in BCs and drysuits changes not just with pressure (depth) but also with temperature variations during a dive.

Tank Pressure Variations:
Expect measurable drops in tank pressure after filling (as tanks cool) and during dives involving thermoclines. Always plan gas reserves with temperature effects in mind.

Controlled Ascents and Lift Bag Management:
During ascents, gas expansion accelerates due to decreasing pressure. Understanding how temperature and volume interact prevents runaway lift bag incidents and unsafe buoyancy changes.

Master divers intuitively blend these gas laws during every dive. They predict how their BC will respond to depth changes, account for tank pressure losses due to cooling, manage their ascent rates carefully, and make real-time decisions based on how gas behavior changes moment by moment. True mastery in diving means seeing these laws not as isolated facts memorized for an exam, but as a living system that guides safe and confident underwater behavior.

Conclusion: From Knowledge to Mastery

Understanding each individual gas law is important, but true diving mastery comes from seeing them as parts of a greater whole. Pressure, volume, temperature, and gas absorption are not isolated facts; they are constantly interacting forces that shape every dive. By internalizing these relationships, divers move beyond rote memorization and gain the ability to anticipate, adapt, and respond intelligently to the underwater environment. Mastery of gas theory transforms diving from a mechanical activity into a confident, informed practice — the true hallmark of a skilled diver.
