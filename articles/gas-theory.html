Gas Theory and Scuba Diving

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

When we descend beneath the waves, we are not just exploring the underwater world — we are entering a realm where gas physics governs every moment of the dive...

What Is Gas Theory?

Gas theory refers to the scientific study of how gases behave under different conditions of pressure, temperature, and volume.

There are several gas theory laws that are specifically applicable to scuba diving. In this setting, we will unpack each and apply the theory to our dive planning process. Along the way, hopefully, we will see the laws fo gasses not as a pedogical exercise needed to score a passing mark on an e-learning course for a needed certification card. Instead, hopefully we see the gas laws as important tools that we have available to assist us inour dive.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures and its Importance in Diving

Dalton’s Law states that the total pressure exerted by a mixture of gases is equal to the sum of the pressures of each individual gas. Each gas in a mixture behaves independently, and its contribution to the total pressure is proportional to its concentration. In diving, this principle becomes crucial because the deeper a diver descends, the higher the absolute pressure, and therefore the higher the partial pressure of each gas the diver breathes.

One of the most critical applications of Dalton’s Law for divers is monitoring the partial pressure of oxygen (PPO2). Oxygen toxicity becomes a significant risk when the PPO2 exceeds 1.4 ata during the working portion of a dive. Therefore, divers must understand how to calculate their maximum operating depth (MOD) based on the oxygen content of their breathing gas.

Maximum Operating Depth (MOD) formula:
MOD = [(PPO2 Limit) ÷ (Fraction of O2)] - 1 ATA

Example for EANx32:
MOD = [(1.4 ÷ 0.32) - 1] × 33 = 111 feet (saltwater)

For example, when diving with EANx32 (32% oxygen), the MOD for a PPO2 limit of 1.4 ata in seawater is approximately 111 feet. For EANx36, the MOD becomes shallower, around 95 feet. This calculation is vital because exceeding the PPO2 limit can lead to central nervous system (CNS) oxygen toxicity, potentially causing convulsions underwater.

It’s a common misconception that diving with Nitrox extends a diver’s maximum depth. It does not. In fact, Nitrox generally reduces the allowable depth compared to air. The true advantage of Nitrox (EANx) is that it reduces the percentage of nitrogen a diver absorbs per breath, which extends no-decompression limits (NDLs). However, Nitrox does not eliminate nitrogen narcosis. The narcosis effect is primarily related to depth and pressure rather than the specific mix of nitrogen and oxygen.

Nitrogen Narcosis Quick Facts

When does it start?
Most divers experience the effects of nitrogen narcosis when the partial pressure of nitrogen (PpN₂) reaches between 3.2 and 3.5 ATA.

What depth does that occur?

  • Air (79% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin between 100 and 120 feet (30–36 meters).
  • EANx32 (68% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin slightly deeper, around 120 to 135 feet (36–41 meters).
  • EANx36 (64% nitrogen): Narcosis may begin around 125 to 140 feet (38–43 meters).

Although Nitrox (EANx32/EANx36) reduces the percentage of nitrogen and may slightly delay the onset of narcosis, the practical depth limits for Nitrox divers are much shallower due to oxygen toxicity concerns. For example, the maximum operating depth (MOD) of EANx36 at a PPO2 limit of 1.4 ATA is approximately 96 feet — far shallower than the depths where narcosis typically becomes a risk. Thus, divers using Nitrox are generally limited by oxygen exposure long before nitrogen narcosis becomes a major concern.

Does Nitrox prevent narcosis?
No. Nitrox does not eliminate narcosis. While it may slightly reduce risk at moderate depths, divers must remain aware that narcosis can still occur, and other factors like depth, total pressure, and environmental stressors play a significant role.

What factors make it worse?
Cold, stress, fatigue, carbon dioxide buildup, alcohol consumption (even from the night before), and anxiety can all lower a diver’s tolerance to narcosis.

Competent divers should be able to compute their MOD based solely on their PPO2 limit and the oxygen content of their breathing gas. Mastery of this fundamental concept is a cornerstone of safe Nitrox diving and is essential for planning any dive that involves enriched air mixtures.

Boyle’s Law and Its Application in Diving

Boyle’s Law describes the relationship between pressure and volume for a gas at constant temperature: as pressure increases, volume decreases proportionally, and vice versa.

For divers, the basic expression — P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ — becomes easy to memorize when studying for a certification test. However, true mastery of diving physics requires understanding how Boyle’s Law affects real-world dive operations and buoyancy control.

Buoyancy during a dive is directly influenced by Boyle’s Law. As a diver descends, external pressure increases, causing the gas in the buoyancy compensator (BC) bladder and the wetsuit’s neoprene material to compress. The diver becomes less buoyant and must add gas to the BC to compensate. During ascent, the opposite occurs: gas expands, increasing buoyancy. If uncontrolled, this expansion can accelerate ascent rates dangerously.

Boyle’s Law also plays a critical role during the recovery of objects underwater. When using a lift bag to raise a submerged object, divers must understand that as the bag rises, the air inside it expands rapidly. This expansion displaces more water, greatly increasing the lift force. If not managed carefully, the object can shoot upward with increasing speed, posing a serious risk to any diver positioned above or below it. Worse, if the bag breaches the surface and flips over, it can lose its air, causing the object to freefall uncontrollably back to the bottom — endangering anyone nearby.

The better diver is not merely someone who memorizes Boyle’s Law for a test but one who internalizes the principle and can apply it confidently in practical scenarios.

Mastery means anticipating how gas volume and buoyancy will change with depth, adjusting for it proactively, and making sound decisions that protect both the diver and the dive team during operations such as lift bag recoveries, deep dives, or safety stops.
