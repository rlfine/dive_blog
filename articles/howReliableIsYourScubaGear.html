How Reliable Is Your Scuba Gear

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

Applying Engineering Reliability Principles to Dive Safety

In the world of scuba diving, we often talk about redundancy and equipment checks—but how often do we step back and treat our dive rig like a critical life-support system? Drawing from reliability engineering principles used in industrial systems, we can gain powerful insights into how the components of our dive gear interact, and what our true system-level risk looks like.

Mean Time to Failure (MTTF): A Misunderstood Metric

MTTF is a statistical average. When a regulator is rated for 100 hours MTTF, that doesn't mean it will fail at hour 100. It means that on average—over many units and conditions—a failure occurs every 100 hours of use. Some fail early, some much later. This makes every dive a roll of the probabilistic dice.

Your Dive Rig as a Series System

Let's define a typical single-tank recreational setup with no redundant first stage. This includes:

  • Tank valve
  • First stage regulator
  • Primary second stage
  • Octopus (cold standby)
  • LP inflator
  • Pressure gauge (SPG)
  • 4 hoses (2 LP, 1 HP, 1 spare)

In engineering, this is a series system: if any one component fails, the system fails.

Reliability Analysis

We converted each component's MTTF into a failure rate (λ), then summed them to get the system-level failure rate.

Tank valve: λ = 0.0001
First stage: λ = 0.002
Primary second stage: λ = 0.01
Octopus (10% on-demand failure): λ = 0.001
LP inflator: λ = 0.0033
SPG: λ = 0.001
Hoses (total): λ = 0.00267
------------------------------
Total system λ = 0.02007 → MTTF ≈ 49.8 hours
Your dive system, under realistic assumptions, has a statistical mean time to failure of just under 50 hours.
What If Your Octopus Is Neglected?

If your backup second stage (octopus) is poorly maintained and has a 50% chance of working on demand:

Octopus λ = 0.01 × 0.5 = 0.005
New total λ = 0.02407 → MTTF ≈ 41.5 hours

That's nearly a 20% drop in system reliability, just from a backup reg being neglected.

Cold Standby ≠ Reliable

An unused component, like the octopus, can suffer from dry O-rings, internal corrosion, or salt deposits—making it unreliable unless actively tested and maintained. You can't depend on it in an emergency unless it's cared for like your primary.

Recommendations for Divers
  • Test your octopus on every dive (purge and check for bubbles).
  • Service all second stages at the same interval—whether used or not.
  • Track gear usage hours, and consider MTTF when planning service intervals.
The Bigger Picture: System Thinking

Treat your dive rig like an engineered system:

  • Understand where redundancy exists—and where it doesn’t.
  • Model reliability, not just functionality.
  • Plan maintenance and testing intervals with failure rates in mind.
Redundancy isn't safety unless it's maintained, tested, and trusted.
Sidebar: MTTF and the Risks of Rental Gear
Rental scuba gear at resorts often sees 3–4 dives per day, especially in saltwater conditions. While convenient, this equipment is under far more stress than gear owned and carefully maintained by occasional recreational divers.

Key concerns with high-use rental gear:

  • Salt buildup: Saltwater dries inside BCD inflators, and over time, if not throughly rinsed, the buildup will cause sticking or free-flowing valves.
  • High wear rate: Regulators, second stages, and inflators reach their MTTF thresholds faster due to continuous cycles of wet/dry, pressure exposure, and handling. Second stage regulators are typically the ball at the end of the rope, banging and knocking on everything
  • Delayed maintenance: Depending on the dive shop, some operators may, from time to time, delay servicing to save costs, especially for backup gear. Delays in service intervals are among the biggest factor in debris accumulation

If you're renting:

  • Test the inflator valve thoroughly—inflate/deflate multiple times before water entry.
  • Check all hoses and connections for leaks or cracks.
  • Breath through both second stages (primary and octopus), purging both.
  • Confirm SPG reads correctly and that the tank is fully pressurized before descent.
You are not wrong to assume a greater responsibility when it comes to a thorough inspection of rental gear in your pre-dive routine—don't let the convenience of traveling light compromise your safety.
