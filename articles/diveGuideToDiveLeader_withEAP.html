From Dive Guide to Dive Leader
Building Your Own Emergency Response Plan

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

Motivation: I completed the PADI Rescue Diver early in my dive progression—shortly after the PADI Advanced Open Water program and prerequisite courses from DAN, including Basic Life Support: CPR and First Aid, and Diving First Aid for Professional Divers. More recently, I’ve begun transitioning from PADI to NAUI, not only for personal development but to support my regular dive buddies—particularly family members like my grandchildren. DAN certifications renew every two years, and this renewal cycle gave me the opportunity to re-engage deeply with rescue concepts as I prepare for the NAUI Rescue Diver course this summer.

Introduction

When diving on cruise excursions, liveaboards, or group charters—especially in places like the Caribbean—we tend to offload all safety and emergency planning to dive guides and boat crews. They maintain the oxygen kits. They activate the emergency action plan. They call EMS. We simply dive.

But that changes when a couple of recreational divers with 50–100 logged dives decide to meet at the local lake or quarry. Who checks the oxygen kit? Who knows where the nearest hospital is? Are we ready to act if the worst happens?

PADI vs. NAUI Rescue Diver: A Quick Comparison

PADI focuses on task-based modules that emphasize surface scenarios, missing diver protocols, and panicked diver response. The structure is highly modular and performance-oriented.

NAUI incorporates the same skills but also emphasizes team-based response, environmental adaptation, and judgment in ambiguous situations. NAUI's philosophy tends to be more holistic, aligning closely with leadership roles and teaching foundations.

Checklist Before Every Independent Dive

  • Do we have a site-specific Emergency Action Plan (EAP), and have we reviewed it together?
  • Is our Emergency Oxygen Kit present, functional, and accessible?
  • Are we able to recognize and respond to symptoms of DCS or AGE?
  • Have we reviewed our First Aid Kit for completeness?
  • Is there an AED on-site or in the vehicle, and do we know how to use it?
  • Do we have mobile phones with EMS contacts saved?
  • Do we know the route and travel time to the nearest hospital?
  • Are we currently certified in CPR and Rescue Breathing?
  • Are we comfortable treating heat illness, stings, bites, or bleeding injuries?

Case Study and Response Scenarios

Emergency Action Plan (EAP)

Each dive outing should begin with a verbal review of the EAP. This includes who will call 911, who will retrieve the O2 kit, who will lead the evacuation to the hospital, and who will perform first aid. Write it down, laminate it, and keep it in your dive log or dry box.

Emergency Oxygen Kit

Your O2 kit should have a demand valve or constant flow regulator, oxygen tank with at least 15 minutes capacity at 15 lpm, and a non-rebreather mask. Check pressure and function before every outing. Oxygen is your most important first aid tool in any diving emergency.

Recognizing DCI: DCS and AGE

Learn to recognize fatigue, joint pain, confusion, blurred vision, and unusual behavior as possible signs of decompression illness. Emergency response includes administering oxygen, recording onset time, keeping the diver horizontal, and notifying DAN or EMS immediately.

First-Class First Aid Kit

A good dive-ready kit includes trauma shears, sterile gauze, antiseptic wipes, burn gel, adhesive bandages, splints, antihistamines, and emergency blankets. Customize for your environment—quarries may need more trauma supplies, while ocean diving may require sting relief products.

AED and Cardiac Emergencies

Sudden cardiac arrest can occur even in fit divers. AEDs should be kept in a waterproof case near shore or in your vehicle. If using one, dry the chest and follow the voice prompts. Early defibrillation saves lives, especially when combined with continuous CPR.

Hospital and EMS Navigation

Before you dive, locate the nearest emergency care facility. Save the address and phone number to your contacts. If remote, know how to summon air evacuation or direct EMS. Time lost finding a hospital is time that could make a difference.

Cuts, Strokes, and Snake Bites

For minor lacerations, clean the wound thoroughly and dress it. In hot environments, recognize heat stroke through confusion and cessation of sweating; immediately cool the diver and hydrate.

For snake bites, immobilize the limb, keep the diver calm, and transport urgently—do not use tourniquets or attempt to suck venom.

Every dive buddy team has the potential to lead. Preparing for the emergency you hope never happens is part of becoming a true dive leader—even if no one has the title.

Dive smart. Dive safe. Be ready.
