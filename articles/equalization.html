Equalize Early, Equalize Thoroughly
Not All Pressure Pain is the Same

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

When scuba diving, few sensations are more familiar—or more risky—than the sudden pressure felt in your ears or forehead. Yet not all pressure sensations are the same, and not every case of discomfort should be treated with the same maneuver. Learning to distinguish between middle ear pressure and sinus squeeze can prevent injury, preserve your dive, and improve your overall diving experience.

The Ears: Frequent but Manageable

The middle ears are the most common source of discomfort. Most divers learn the Valsalva maneuver—pinching the nose and blowing gently—to equalize ear pressure. But Valsalva is not foolproof. If attempted too late, or with excessive force, it can cause further injury such as barotrauma or even rupture the eardrum. Other techniques, like the Frenzel maneuver (using the throat muscles to push air upward) or the Toynbee maneuver (swallowing while pinching the nose), are often more effective and safer during gradual descents.

Safety Tip: Always use gentle pressure when equalizing. Forceful Valsalva attempts can worsen blockages or rupture sensitive membranes.

The Sinuses: The Underestimated Source

Pressure pain above the eyebrows, behind the cheeks, or deep within the forehead usually points to sinus squeeze, not ear trouble. The sinus cavities must also equalize during descent, but their narrow passages are prone to blockage from mucus, inflammation, or congestion. Attempting to force a Valsalva maneuver against blocked sinuses can make matters worse.

Safe Sinus Clearing Techniques

One effective strategy for addressing sinus pressure involves clearing the nasal passages during the dive. A method supported by ENT and diving medicine sources involves:

  • Blowing gently through one nostril at a time while underwater, using your mask to contain the water and air.
  • Hold one nostril closed, blow firmly but not violently through the open side.
  • Repeat on the other side.
Note: Gentle, directed nasal clearing can promote sinus drainage without risking damage to delicate tissues. Never attempt to forcefully "blast" your sinuses clear.

Why Early and Gentle Equalization Matters

Waiting until you feel pressure before trying to equalize is a mistake. Equalize early—before discomfort—and continue to equalize every few feet during your descent. If you encounter resistance, stop, ascend a few feet, and attempt again using gentler methods. Force is never the answer.

Pro Tip: Start equalizing even before you descend to maintain a slight positive pressure in your ears and sinuses from the beginning.

Know When to Abort

If equalization—whether in the ears or sinuses—remains difficult despite multiple gentle attempts, it’s critical to recognize that persisting may lead to serious injury. Abort the dive if necessary. Your safety and long-term health are far more important than pushing through a preventable injury.

Final Tips

  • Start equalizing at the surface before descending.
  • Equalize every few feet, not just when you feel pain.
  • Use multiple techniques: Valsalva, Frenzel, Toynbee, and sinus clearing.
  • Stay well hydrated and avoid diving with congestion.
  • Listen to your body: pain is a warning, not a challenge.

Dive smart. Dive safe. Dive often.
