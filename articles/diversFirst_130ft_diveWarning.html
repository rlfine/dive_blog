The Recreational 40 Meter Limit?
Should you, why, and how to prepare

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

If You’re Planning Your First Dive to 130 Feet—And You’re Not At Least a Little Bit Worried—You’re Not Ready

That might sound harsh, but it’s not a criticism of your curiosity or courage. It’s a reflection of how serious deep diving really is—and how often divers mistake supervision for safety.

I’ve met many dive professionals, a few fall into the excellent category, and I trust a small subset with my life. But no dive pro, no matter how skilled, can substitute for your own readiness—mentally, physically, and emotionally.

Depth doesn’t just test your training; it tests your judgment, your equipment, your breathing discipline, and your ability to stay calm when things start to go sideways.

A little healthy fear at 130 feet is a sign that your survival instinct is working. It means you’re thinking about your narcosis threshold, your gas supply, your suit compression, and your ability to manage yourself if things change quickly.

1. Narcosis Is Not a Maybe—It’s a Certainty

At 130 feet, narcosis isn’t something you might feel. It’s something you will feel. The effects range from mild tunnel vision to full-blown mental fog, confusion, or inappropriate confidence.

If you don’t recognize it—it’s already affecting you.

2. Your Buddy Isn’t Your Backup—They’re Narced Too

The buddy system assumes someone can help if things go wrong. But at 130 feet, both of you are affected by narcosis. If your buddy panics, misses a detail, or forgets a skill—they’re not your lifeline. You have to plan assuming you’ll need to manage yourself and help them if necessary.

3. That 7mm Suit Isn’t 7mm Anymore

If you are diving in a deep lake, or anywhere the water is cold, thermal protection becomes key factor. Diving in cold water at 30 or 40 feet with a 5mm or even a 7mm wetsuit is a relatively easy skill to master.

Neoprene compresses. What keeps you warm at 50 feet might become the thermal equivalent of a rash guard at 130. A cold diver is a distracted diver—and that distraction burns gas, clouds judgment, and speeds up fatigue. If you dive deep in a wetsuit, layer intelligently and expect thermal loss.

Neoprene compression impacts buoyancy as well. You might need upwards of 30 pounds of lead to descend from the surface in a 7mm wetsuit. But Boyle's law works on the air cells in the neoprene wetsuit same as it does on the lift bag, the SMB, your lungs, and the gas you breathe. When the 7mm wetsuits compresses down to something less than a 3mm wetsuit at 130 feet, you will suddenly find yourself 20-30 pounds over weighted. If you have an equipment failure and need to jetison some weight, you might find yourself on a fast elevator to the surface - racing past any intended deep stop and especially racing past the mandatory safety stop.

4. Your Gas Is Thicker, and Your Time Is Shorter

At five atmospheres, every breath takes five times the surface gas volume—and the increased density makes each breath more effortful. That combination means you’ll consume gas faster and feel like you’re working harder just to breathe. Plan your gas with a margin, and practice your ascent with awareness.

5. Cold, Dark, and Rising CO₂: The Anxiety Spiral

Deep, dark water. Cold creeping into your core. A rising heart rate. You breathe faster. CO₂ builds. Anxiety rises. And unless you interrupt that feedback loop, it gets worse—not better. Recognizing this cycle before it starts is critical. Breath control and pre-dive mindfulness are your best defenses.

Deep diving isn’t about pushing your limits. It’s about knowing them. If you're not at least a little bit concerned before your first 130-foot dive, then you’re not thinking clearly about what depth really demands. Respect the depth. Prepare for the dive. And never let ego take the lead.
