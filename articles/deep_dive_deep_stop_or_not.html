Deep Dive Mid-level Safety Stops

Ascent Strategy for Deep Dives: Why Mid-Depth Stops Matter More Than You Think

When recreational divers first learn to dive, they are taught the fundamentals of a safe ascent: ascend no faster than 30 feet per minute and make a 3-minute safety stop at 15–20 feet before surfacing. This simple protocol is highly effective for dives within the typical recreational range of 30–60 feet. But as divers advance—venturing deeper and staying longer—the risks shift, and so should your ascent strategy.

In this post, we’ll explore why a more nuanced approach—specifically adding a short mid-depth stop around half your maximum depth—can provide added protection on deeper dives. We’ll also look at the science behind gas absorption, the role of different tissue compartments, and how deeper stops fit into a more complete decompression picture.

Understanding Nitrogen Loading: Fast vs. Slow Tissues

When you breathe compressed air underwater, nitrogen dissolves into your body tissues. Some tissues—like blood and lung-adjacent tissues—absorb and release nitrogen quickly. These are called fast compartments. Others, such as fat, nerves, and connective tissue, absorb and release nitrogen much more slowly; these are the slow compartments.

Most decompression models, like the classic Haldanian and Bühlmann models, assume these compartments are all exposed directly to the same ambient pressure. However, this simplification doesn't fully reflect human physiology. The Canadian DCIEM (Defence and Civil Institute of Environmental Medicine) model, for example, adds a key insight: while fast tissues are exposed directly to ambient pressure, slow tissues are only indirectly exposed, exchanging nitrogen through the blood. This “layered” model more closely mimics how nitrogen actually moves through the body.

Why the Standard Safety Stop Works—But Isn't Always Enough

The standard 3-minute safety stop at 15–20 feet is crucial because it allows the nitrogen-saturated fast compartments to off-gas at a pressure where bubbles are small and manageable. For shallow dives, this is usually sufficient. However, as depth increases, the amount of nitrogen absorbed—especially in slow tissues—rises significantly. On a dive to 110 feet for 18 minutes, for example, fast compartments may be near full saturation, while slow tissues are still loading nitrogen as you begin your ascent.

Ascending directly from depth to 15 feet creates a large pressure reduction in a short time. While your dive computer might show you're within no-decompression limits, the rapid pressure change can allow microbubbles to form or expand, increasing decompression stress.

The Role of the Mid-Depth Stop

By adding a brief pause at around half your maximum depth—say, 1 to 3 minutes at 55 feet after a 110-foot dive—you create a staged ascent. This lets fast tissues begin off-gassing earlier, reducing their nitrogen load before you hit the shallow zone. In the DCIEM model, this also lowers nitrogen pressure in the blood, which improves the gradient for deeper (slow) tissues to start off-gassing more effectively during the final safety stop.

The benefit? By shedding nitrogen in stages, you stabilize microbubbles early and set up your body for more efficient overall decompression.

What the Science Says

Early studies and bubble models like RGBM (Reduced Gradient Bubble Model) and VPM (Variable Permeability Model) strongly supported deep stops, suggesting that keeping pressure higher during ascent helps reduce bubble formation. More recent research, including the 2011 NEDU (Navy Experimental Diving Unit) study, has shown that prolonged deep stops can delay off-gassing in slow tissues, which may actually increase decompression risk if not managed carefully.

However, it’s important to distinguish between deep stops (extended pauses at depths much deeper than half the dive depth) and brief mid-depth stops. The latter—brief pauses around half your max depth—strike a balance: they allow fast tissues to begin off-gassing without unnecessarily prolonging total ascent time or delaying the final, most important shallow stop.

Best Practices for Deep Recreational Dives

  • Ascend slowly: No faster than 30 feet per minute (and slower if possible, especially above 50 feet).
  • Add a mid-depth stop: For dives deeper than 80 feet, pause for 1–3 minutes at about half your maximum depth.
  • Don’t skip the final stop: Make a full 3–5 minute stop at 15–20 feet. If you have gas and time, longer shallow stops (up to 10 minutes) add extra safety margin.
  • Plan conservatively: Stay well within your no-decompression limits and be mindful of repetitive dives and environmental stressors like cold water or exertion.

The Takeaway

For dives in the 30–40 foot range, the standard safety stop is typically more than adequate. But as you venture deeper and extend bottom times, your decompression strategy should evolve. Adding a brief mid-depth stop is a simple, effective way to manage nitrogen off-gassing more safely and reduce decompression stress. Backed by physiological models like DCIEM, this approach emphasizes that deeper diving requires deeper thinking—and a commitment to ascent discipline that goes beyond your first open water class.

Remember: when in doubt, slow down, stop, and breathe. Your body—and your future dives—will thank you.
