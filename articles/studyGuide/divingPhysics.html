Foundational Scuba Diving Knowledge

We live at the bottom of an ocean of air. We are adapted to these surroundings. Our bodies, our senses, our perceptions, our instincts have evolved in response to our terrestrial environment.

There are many properties of the aquatic environment that can have profound, even life threatening effects if we do not understand and compensate for them.

Many persons see physics as a challenging if not a daunting subject. They are not comfortable with numbers and mathematics. Remember, the numbers and the formulas encountered along the way are simply tools, not ends in themselves.

The early scientific discoverers used mathematics to better understand and describe what they were actually observing. We, as divers that are purposely expanding our knowledge and skills, can benefit from the same.

The Dive Physics course of study in the NAUI Master Scuba Diver is intended to extendsour knowledge by further defining concepts and providing the diver with tools for solving diving-related problems. The goal is to provide a better and deeper understanding of the principles of physics as they affect each of us in our diving.

If a diver is neutrally buoyant with 8 lbs of lead in fresh water, they typically need 2–4 additional pounds in salt water due to the increased density. For most divers, this means 10–12 lbs total. The exact amount depends on factors such as wetsuit thickness, tank type, and personal buoyancy.

Determine the object's weight in water by subtracting displaced water weight from dry weight. Since each liter of displacement provides ~2.2 lbs of lift, a 100 lb object displacing 30 liters would weigh 34 lbs underwater, requiring a lift bag rated for at least 40–45 lbs with margin.

A: Tidal Volume (TV) is the amount of air you breathe in or out during a normal, relaxed breath. In a healthy adult at rest, this is typically about 500–700 mL (0.5–0.7 liters) per breath. It plays a key role in estimating respiratory minute volume (RMV), which is important for gas planning in diving. RMV is calculated as Tidal Volume × Breathing Rate, and is used to understand how much gas a diver will consume per minute at the surface.

A: During moderate activity like average-speed walking (~3–4 mph), a healthy adult’s tidal volume typically increases to around 1.0 to 1.5 liters per breath. This is a natural response to the body's higher oxygen demand and increased carbon dioxide production. Alongside a breathing rate of 20–30 breaths per minute, this results in a Respiratory Minute Volume (RMV) of approximately 20–45 liters per minute — a substantial increase from resting values.

A: At 90 feet (27 meters) in saltwater, the ambient pressure is 4 ATA (1 atm atmospheric + 3 atm water).

A diver’s lungs still inhale the same physical volume per breath (e.g., 0.6–0.9 L), but the gas is 4× denser, so each breath contains 4× the number of molecules compared to the surface.

That means each breath uses 2.4–3.6 L of surface-equivalent air. With a moderate breathing rate of 15–20 breaths per minute, the diver may consume 36–72 L of surface air per minute.

This highlights why deeper dives require careful gas planning and why understanding tidal volume is essential in dive physiology.

The coolest water temperature most divers can be reasonably comfortable in with no thermal protection (just a bathing suit) is around:

▶️ 78–82°F (25–28°C)

This range represents the lower limit of comfort for short to medium duration recreational dives. Below this, most divers begin to feel chilled or even cold very quickly.

Water Temp Comfort Level (Swimsuit Only) Notes
>82°F / >28°C Very comfortable Tropical “bathwater” conditions
78–82°F / 25–28°C Barely comfortable for short dives Some divers may feel chilled by end of dive
75–77°F / 24–25°C Chilly, tolerable ~30 min Noticeable heat loss, mild discomfort
<75°F / <24°C Uncomfortable to hypothermic Risk of cold stress increases rapidly

⚠️ Important: Individual physiology matters. Lean divers, older adults, or those with low activity levels chill faster. Cold water draws heat from your body ~25x faster than air.

💡 Rule of thumb: If you would be shivering after floating in it for 20 minutes, you probably need at least a thin wetsuit.

  • Starting Pressure: 3540 psi → ~3307 liters total air
  • RMV: 24 liters/min

Dive Segments and Air Use:

  • Descent to 82 ft (1.49 min @ 2.41 ATA): 86.1 L
  • Bottom time at 82 ft:
    • Air used from start to 920 psi = 2598 L total
    • Subtract descent = 2598 − 86.1 = 2511.9 L
    • Ambient pressure at 82 ft = 3.49 ATA
    • Time at depth = 2511.9 ÷ (24 × 3.49) ≈ 30.0 min
  • Ascent to 50 ft (1.6 min @ 2.56 ATA): 98.2 L
  • Stop at 50 ft (5 min @ 2.52 ATA): 302.4 L
  • Ascent to 15 ft (1.17 min @ 1.75 ATA): 49.1 L
  • Stop at 15 ft (5 min @ 1.45 ATA): 174.0 L
  • Final ascent (0.5 min @ 1.23 ATA): 14.8 L

Total Air Used: 86.1 + 2511.9 + 98.2 + 302.4 + 49.1 + 174.0 + 14.8 = 3236.5 liters

Remaining Air: 3307 − 3236.5 = 70.5 liters

Ending Pressure: (70.5 / 3307) × 3540 ≈ ~75 psi

Total Dive Time: 1.49 + 30.0 + 1.6 + 5.0 + 1.17 + 5.0 + 0.5 = ~44.8 minutes

⚠️ Caution: This dive scenario ends with an estimated ~70 liters (75 psi) remaining in a steel HP120 tank. This is well below the generally recommended minimum reserve of 500 psi (or ~10–15% of starting pressure).

Most modern first and second stage regulators are not designed to operate reliably at cylinder pressures below 150–200 psi. As tank pressure drops below this critical range—especially at depth—regulator performance may degrade rapidly, or even fail to deliver breathable gas.

Implication: Surfacing with 75 psi may not merely violate gas management best practices—it could represent the onset of an out-of-air emergency in the final phase of the dive.

Insufficient reserve jeopardizes:

  • Emergency gas sharing (for self or buddy)
  • Buoyant ascent control
  • Safety stop completion

Best practice: Plan your dive to reach the surface with a reserve of at least 500–700 psi. Treat this buffer as untouchable emergency gas, not as usable dive time.
