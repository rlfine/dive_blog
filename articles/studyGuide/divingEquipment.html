Foundational Scuba Diving Knowledge

As divers transition from rental equipment to their own gear, a solid foundation in dive equipment coupled with more than just a passing familiarity of dive physics is necessary

As a certified diver, you are familiar with the basic equipment used for diving.

As a NAUI Master Scuba Diver you need to know more about the equipment you use and equipment you might consider purchasing. You should also know about standard recreational equipment such as masks, regulators, dive computers, dry suits, and air tanks.

Scuba diving apparatus includes open-circuit demand systems (standard scuba tanks and regulators), semi-closed circuit rebreathers (SCR), and closed-circuit rebreathers (CCR). Open-circuit systems release exhaled gas, while SCR and CCR recycle breathing gas, improving efficiency and extending dive time.

Steel tanks are typically more negatively buoyant and compact than aluminum tanks. They are less likely to become positively buoyant when near empty, reducing the amount of lead a diver must carry.
However, steel tanks are more susceptible to rust, requiring careful maintenance.

Proper care includes annual visual inspections (VIP), hydrostatic testing every 5 years, and periodic tumbling to remove internal corrosion.
Tanks should always be stored with some pressure (300–500 psi) to prevent moisture intrusion.

Tank markings include the material code (e.g., 3AL for aluminum), service pressure (e.g., 3000 psi), serial number, manufacturer ID, and the hydrostatic test date. Retest symbols indicate when and where the tank was last hydro tested.

The AL80 (aluminum 80) is the most common tank, with a service pressure of 3000 psi, a capacity of ~77.4 cu ft (~2190 liters), and becomes positively buoyant when near empty. Steel HP100 and HP120 tanks operate at 3442 psi, offer higher gas capacity, and are negatively buoyant even when empty.

Scuba tank valves include Yoke (INT) and DIN types. DIN valves can handle higher pressures and are preferred for technical diving. K-valves are standard modern valves, while older J-valves had a built-in reserve mechanism. Avoid galvanic corrosion by separating dissimilar metals and rinsing gear post-dive.

Diaphragm regulators isolate internal components from water, offering reliability in cold or dirty environments. Piston regulators can deliver higher air flow but may expose internal parts to water. Both types come in balanced and unbalanced designs affecting performance under pressure.

Balanced second stages maintain consistent breathing effort regardless of tank pressure. Unbalanced regulators may require more effort as pressure drops. Pilot valve or servo-assisted designs enhance breathing ease but may add complexity and cost.

After each dive, rinse regulators in fresh water with the dust cap securely in place. Avoid pressing the purge button while rinsing. Annual servicing by a certified technician is recommended to maintain safe and reliable performance.

Some regulators struggle to deliver high airflow at low tank pressures, which can be critical if two divers share air. Balanced, environmentally sealed regulators with high-performance second stages are best for deep or cold water diving and emergencies.

Analog gauges use a mechanical needle to indicate tank pressure and are durable and simple. Digital gauges provide a precise numerical readout and may include backlighting or integration with dive computers, but require batteries and can be more fragile.

Depth gauges may be analog (open or sealed bourdon tube) or digital.
Oil-filled bourdon gauges reduce friction and increase reliability.
Digital versions are typically more accurate and easier to read but require power and can fail electronically.

Dive computers track real-time depth and time, calculate nitrogen absorption using decompression models, and extend dive times compared to tables. They alert divers to ascent rates, no-fly times, and mandatory decompression stops.

Analog compasses are reliable, require no power, and work in all conditions. Digital compasses may be integrated with dive computers and offer easier reading, but can fail if batteries die or electronics malfunction.

  • Jacket-style BCDs wrap around the diver and are commonly used in training.
  • Wing-style BCDs (back inflation) provide better horizontal trim and are preferred for technical diving.
Both BCD types may have integrated weights, dump valves, and require rinsing after use.

Protection prevents hypothermia and increases comfort.
Divers typically use the following (depending on preferences and temperature sensitivity):

  • rash guards in tropical waters (>82°F),
  • 3mm wetsuits in warm water (75–82°F),
  • 5mm wetsuits in moderate water (65–75°F),
  • 7mm wetsuits in cold water (55–65°F), and
  • drysuits in very cold water (<55°F).

Rust consumes oxygen and can weaken the tank walls. Rust particles may clog regulator filters or valves. Regular inspection and proper drying/storage of steel tanks can prevent these risks.

Dive computers continuously recalculate nitrogen loading based on real-time depth, unlike dive tables which assume a square profile. This results in more accurate and often more generous no-decompression limits.

The two main types are wrist-mounted and console-mounted. Some are air-integrated, providing tank pressure and air time remaining. Others are basic models that track time, depth, and nitrogen loading.

If your computer fails, abort the dive and ascend at a safe rate, performing a safety stop. Always dive with a backup timing and depth device or plan dives conservatively using tables.

Underwater timers are easier to read, often larger, and may include additional functions like alarms. They're better suited for technical or backup use compared to casual dive watches.

Balanced regulators maintain consistent intermediate pressure regardless of tank pressure, resulting in easier breathing at depth or low tank pressure. Unbalanced regulators are simpler and less expensive, but breathing resistance may increase as tank pressure drops.

Environmental sealing prevents water and contaminants from entering the first stage, which reduces the risk of freezing in cold water and corrosion over time. This makes sealed regulators ideal for cold or contaminated environments.

The purge button manually opens the second stage valve, allowing water to be cleared from the mouthpiece. It can also assist in testing airflow before entering the water.

These regulators use a two-stage mechanism where a small pilot valve activates a larger main valve. This allows for extremely low breathing resistance, often favored in high-performance or technical regulators.

Signs include inconsistent airflow, bubbles near hose connections, increased breathing effort, and unusual noises. Annual servicing or after extensive use is recommended even if no symptoms are evident.

Intermediate pressure is the pressure between the first and second stages, typically 120–150 psi above ambient. It is critical to performance and is adjusted during servicing to match manufacturer specifications.

High-pressure ports (HP) are used for pressure gauges and transmit tank pressure. Low-pressure ports (LP) supply second stages, BCD inflators, and drysuits, delivering regulated air at intermediate pressure.

Pressing the purge button while the regulator is not pressurized can allow water to enter the hose and first stage. This can cause internal corrosion or contamination, especially if not followed by thorough drying and servicing.

Galvanic corrosion occurs when dissimilar metals, such as stainless steel and aluminum, come into contact in a moist environment. It can be prevented by using compatible materials, isolating contacts, and rinsing gear after saltwater dives.

An overfilled tank will read above its service pressure on a pressure gauge. While some tanks allow overfilling (e.g., +10% in U.S. DOT 'plus-rated' cylinders), consistent overfilling can reduce tank lifespan or cause rupture.

Tumbling involves rotating a tank with abrasive media inside to remove internal rust and corrosion. It restores the tank's interior surface and prepares it for reinspection or hydrostatic testing.

Using an untested tank increases the risk of rupture due to unseen corrosion or metal fatigue. Dive shops often refuse to fill out-of-date tanks for safety and liability reasons.

A hydrostatic test assesses the tank’s ability to safely hold pressure by filling it with water and pressurizing it beyond service pressure. It is typically required every 5 years for scuba cylinders.

A J-valve included a mechanical reserve feature that restricted air flow when tank pressure dropped to ~300 psi. Pulling a lever restored flow, alerting the diver it was time to ascend. Modern regulators and SPGs have largely replaced J-valves.

An SPG displays the pressure remaining in a scuba tank. It is connected to the high-pressure port of a regulator and allows divers to monitor air supply throughout the dive.

A console typically holds an SPG, depth gauge, compass, and sometimes a dive computer. It attaches via a high-pressure hose and keeps multiple instruments in one accessible cluster.

Overpressure relief valves vent gas if intermediate pressure exceeds safe limits, protecting downstream hoses and second stages from rupture due to regulator malfunction.

Water capacity measures the internal volume in liters, while gas volume refers to the amount of air it holds at rated pressure. For example, an AL80 has ~11L water capacity but holds ~2190 liters of air at 3000 psi.

Empty tanks may allow moisture to enter, causing internal corrosion. Storing tanks with 300–500 psi ensures they remain sealed and protected from humidity and contaminants.

Surface gauges are used pre- and post-dive to check tank fills, while SPGs are designed for underwater use and are part of the diver’s equipment during the dive.

A K-valve is the standard modern scuba tank valve that allows free flow of air and requires a submersible pressure gauge to monitor tank pressure. A J-valve, used before SPGs were common, had a built-in mechanical reserve that restricted airflow when pressure dropped to about 300 psi—alerting the diver it was time to ascend.

Integrated weights are stored in pouches built into the BCD, eliminating the need for a separate weight belt. They improve comfort and streamline the diver's profile but must be quick-release in case of emergency ditching. Divers should regularly practice weight pouch removal and reattachment.

A quality BCD typically has two or three dump valves: one at the right shoulder, one at the rear lower corner, and sometimes a third at the left shoulder near the inflator. These allow controlled air release in various diving positions for fine-tuned buoyancy control.

Rinse the exterior thoroughly with fresh water, then partially inflate the BCD and rinse the interior by adding water through the oral inflator. Swish the water around and drain it through the dump valves. Dry the BCD partially inflated in the shade to avoid mildew and material degradation.

Salt residue can corrode metal components, stiffen materials, and degrade O-rings or seals over time. Regular rinsing and occasional deep cleaning are essential to extend equipment life and maintain performance.

While wearing full gear and with an empty BCD at the surface, you should float at eye level with a normal breath and sink slowly when exhaling. If you sink too fast, remove weight; if you float too high, add weight. This ensures neutral buoyancy at depth.

DIN valves screw into the regulator, creating a more secure and higher-pressure seal (up to 300 bar). This reduces the risk of O-ring extrusion and accidental disconnection—important in deep, cold, or overhead environments. However, DIN valves require compatible regulators or adapters.

A modular valve manifold connects two tanks with an isolator valve. It allows access to both tanks from either regulator. In case of a failure, the isolator can shut off one tank, preserving gas in the other. It’s standard for technical diving with redundant systems.

Wing-style BCDs have a bladder located behind the diver, promoting a horizontal trim position and are preferred by technical divers. Jacket-style BCDs wrap around the torso, providing upright surface flotation and easier control for beginners. Each has strengths depending on dive style.

Recreational BCDs typically offer 20 to 40 pounds (9 to 18 kg) of lift. Divers using heavy gear, thick wetsuits, or cold-water exposure protection may require BCDs with higher lift capacity to ensure proper buoyancy control and emergency floatation.

Drysuits insulate the diver by keeping an air layer between the body and water, preventing heat loss. They’re essential in water below 55°F (13°C), where wetsuits may not provide adequate thermal protection. Undergarments are worn inside for additional insulation.

Trim weights are small weights placed near the tank or upper body to fine-tune horizontal trim in the water. They help prevent feet-first or head-down positions and improve comfort and efficiency while swimming.

Flying too soon after diving increases the risk of decompression sickness due to lower cabin pressure. Dive computers calculate no-fly times based on residual nitrogen, typically recommending 12 to 24 hours before flying depending on dive profiles.

Recognize that the BCD failure is a condition that requires a calm, deliberate response. Do not panic. This is a situation that can be resolved!
  • Signal to your buddy there is a problem, then signal to end the dive.
  • Ascend using your fins, orally inflate your BCD at the surface.
  • If needed, drop weights to initiate a controlled ascent.
Note: A redundant buoyancy device such as a drysuit or lift bag may be used in technical diving for backup buoyancy.

Any regulator malfunction should be treated as an emergency. If one second stage fails, switch to an alternate air source and end the dive. For dual tank or technical setups, isolate the failure and surface safely.

Divers should carry a waterproof digital timer or dive watch in case the dive computer fails. This backup allows tracking of bottom time and ascent duration, essential for safety and compliance with decompression limits.

Fully inflate the BCD and submerge it in a tub or pool. Look for air bubbles around seams, valves, and the inflator. Also let it sit overnight fully inflated—if it deflates significantly, inspect for pinhole leaks or faulty valves.

Not necessarily. Many second stage regulators will freeflow when they are:

Quick Test Checklist:

  • Turn mouthpiece down — freeflow should stop
  • Tap or press purge — flow should reset and stop
  • Check breathing resistance — should feel smooth, not “on” all the time
  • Compare with same model — this helps rule out tuning issues

When to Get It Checked:

  • Freeflows in any orientation, even mouthpiece down
  • Freeflows at the surface with no water contact
  • Freeflows during a dive without inhalation
  • Behavior changed after recent servicing

If you're concerned, it’s worth taking the regulator to a certified technician for an inspection. But in most cases, this is normal behavior — not a defect.
