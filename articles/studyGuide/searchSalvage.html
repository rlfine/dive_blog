As divers transition from rental equipment to their own gear, a solid foundation in dive physics, physiology, and marine awareness ensures informed decisions and safer dives.

If a diver is neutrally buoyant with 8 lbs of lead in fresh water, they typically need 2–4 additional pounds in salt water due to the increased density. For most divers, this means 10–12 lbs total. The exact amount depends on factors such as wetsuit thickness, tank type, and personal buoyancy.

Venomous creatures inject toxins via sting, bite, or puncture—like lionfish and cone snails. Poisonous animals are harmful if touched or ingested—like certain pufferfish organs. This distinction affects emergency responses and safety precautions.

The first step is to determine the object's volume and dry weight. To determine the objects weight in water, subtract the weight of the displaced water (e.g., the buoyant force) from the object's dry weight.

Since each liter of displacement of saltwater provides ~2.3 lbs of lift, and that lift or buoyancy counters the objects dry weight, we subtract the weight of the displaced water from the dry weight of the object, to find the required lifting force.

For our example, we assume the object has a dry weight of 100 lbs and a volume of 0.44 ft3.

  • Dry weight of object at surface: 45.4 kg (100 lbs)
  • Water displacement of object (volume): 12.4 liters (0.44 ft3)
  • Weight of 1 liter saltwater 1.03 kg (2.3 lb)
  • Weight of 12 liters saltwater: 12.7 kg (~28 lb)
  • Lift required for object in saltwater: 45.4 kg - 12.7 kg = 32.7 KG (100 lb - 28.2 lb = 71.8 lb)
