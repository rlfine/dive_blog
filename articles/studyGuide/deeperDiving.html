Foundational Scuba Diving Knowledge

Deep Diving as defined by NAUI is any dive that occurs between 18m and 40m (60ft and 130ft). Dives beyond 40m (130ft) are beyond the scope and training fopr recreational divers. Deep dives also differ from shallower dives with respect to temperature changes, breathing resistance, reduced light, possible narcosis, additional buoyancy adjustments, and anxiety. A precautionary stop is particularly important at the end of any deep dive; this refers to a non-required decompression stop made as a safety precaution. All but the shallowest dives should incorporate such a stop.

Your guiding principle should be to use the minimum amount of weight which will provide neutral buoyancy at 5 meters (15 feet) of depth with an empty buoyancy compensator and a nearly-empty cylinder. This ensures the ability to execute a precautionary safety stop at 5 meters (15 feet) at the end of a dive without risking premature surfacing.

Attach nothing to your ballast system that is also attached to something else. This could prevent jettisoning your weights in an emergency.

Boyle’s Law states that the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to the pressure on it (at constant temperature).
At depth, air spaces in the body compress—critical for understanding equalization and managing buoyancy.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures: As ambient pressure increases, the partial pressure of nitrogen rises, increasing its narcotic effect on the body..

Redundant air sources (e.g., pony bottle, bailout bottle) are critical. A dive computer with decompression capability and a reliable depth gauge are equa;lly critical to deeper diving. A backup dive computer is recommended.

Air consumption increases proportionally with pressure. At 99 feet (30 meters), you consume about 4 times as much air as at the surface. You must calculate Rock Bottom gas reserves and plan turn-around pressures accordingly.

1.4 ATA is considered the safe maximum for working portions of the dive; 1.6 ATA may be used briefly for decompression stops.

Begin emergency decompression stops per your dive computer or table guidelines.
Example: If you're at 100 feet and exceed the NDL by 5 minutes, perform a deep stop at 50 ft for a few minutes is air supply allows, then ascend to 15 feet and hold a prolonged safety stop (often 8+ minutes), then surface slowly.

Add air gradually and early to your BCD when descending to prevent rapid descents due to suit and BCD compression.
Always anticipate buoyancy changes during ascent and vent your BCD (e.g., dump air) as needed to control speed.

Overweighting adds to your stress in the event of an occurrence requiring extra effort (long surface swim, increased currents, wind, buddy needing assistance, etc.). Additionally, if the BC malfunctions or fails to hold air, an overweighted diver may be in a dangerous situation. If the diver is unable to control descent and is too heavy to ascend, then the weight belt must be ditched, and the diver is faced with dealing with too-rapid bouyant ascent.

Some of the reasons to dive deep may include exploration, photography, and marine life identification. Some people dive deep as an ego challenge. Our egos are the essence of our quest for adventure. A healthy ego gives us confidence, persistence, creativity, and flexibility and is vital to the ability to manage stress. But overconfidence (or an unhealthy ego) can lead to errors in judgment or diving beyond limitations.

For dives in the range of 18 to 40 msw (60 to 130 fsw), primary equipment should be upgraded to include the following:

  • Scuba cylinder with minimum capacity of 2200 L (80 cf).
  • Primary cylinder equipped with a “Y” or “H” valved dual regulators, or a separate pony bottle cylinder (approx. 425 to 850 L [15 to 30 ft3] ) with its own regulator, which can be mounted to the primary cylinder.
  • Two regulators for primary gas source. One may be used on secondary scuba. One regulator is to be fitted with a 100 cm (40 in) hose and the other with a hose that is at least 150 cm (60 in) long.

Your first concern is performance. Will the regulator deliver a sufficient gas volume with minimal breathing effort under high demands at the anticipated maximum dive depth? Keep in mind that your gas flow requirements can more than quadruple in a high stress situation.

If applicable, will the regulator perform adequately in cold water or will it ice (resulting in freeflow) under high demand conditions?

Here are a few guidelines to consider:

  • Plan the dive not to exceed the Maximum Dive Time limits of the NAUI Dive Tables. These time limits are more conservative than the U.S. Navy no-decompression limits. If you are using a dive computer, you may be able to make a multi-level dive that is longer than the squarewave time limits. Check the dive planning mode of your dive computer.
  • Make sure the profile is realistically tailored to the underwater terrain and depth.
  • Select a profile that is feasible, considering the air supply and air consumption rate for both you and your buddy.
  • Take an underwater slate with you, with your primary and contingency profiles written out.
  • Dive your plan.

At the surface, the pressure is 1 ATA. At 40 meters, the pressure is 5 ATA. With a 5x increase in pressure, each breath of air is 5 times denser (e.g., 5 times the number of molecules compared to surface breathing). Therefore, at 40 meters, air is consumed 5 times faster, and the diver would have 30 minutes / 5 minutes or 6 minutes.
