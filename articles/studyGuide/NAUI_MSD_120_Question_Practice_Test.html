The NAUI (National Association of Underwater Instructors) Comprehensive Final Exam is a critical component of the NAUI Open Water Scuba Diver certification process. It assesses your understanding of essential scuba diving principles to ensure you're prepared for safe and responsible diving

The exam evaluates your knowledge across several key areas:

  • Scuba Equipment: Proper use and maintenance of gear.
  • Dive Physics: Understanding gas laws and pressure-related concepts.
  • Physiology: Effects of diving on the human body.
  • Dive Planning: Using dive tables and computers for safe dive profiles.
  • Safety Procedures: Emergency protocols and risk management.
  • Environmental Awareness: Protecting marine ecosystems.

The objective of the comprehensive assessment is to ensure you have a grasp of the theoretical knowledge and are ready to apply that knowledge to real-world diving situations.

Bonus Section

When a diver descends, ambient pressure increases — from 1 ATA at the surface to over 5 ATA at 130 feet. This pressure increase causes nitrogen to dissolve into body tissues, not come out of solution. There is no supersaturation, so there’s no drive for bubble formation.

In fact, any microscopic gas nuclei that do exist (from previous dives or normal physiology) are compressed by the increasing pressure, becoming smaller and more stable. Bubble formation requires a decrease in pressure, not an increase — which is why the ascent is the critical phase, not the descent.

Even if small bubbles or gas nuclei were somehow present during descent, they would not pose a risk. Boyle’s Law tells us that increasing pressure compresses gas volumes. At 130 feet, a bubble would shrink to roughly 1/5 of its surface size. This compression dramatically increases the surface tension and pressure inside the bubble, often causing it to dissolve back into surrounding tissue.

Since tissue damage (such as tearing or inflammation) occurs from bubble expansion, not compression, there is no mechanical disruption or injury from these stabilized or shrinking bubbles. That’s why descent is not dangerous in this respect — it's the ascent, especially if rapid or poorly managed, that causes bubbles to grow and exert physical force on surrounding tissue.

Scenario: A diver has a steel HP100 tank with 1800 psi of EAN32 remaining and wants to top it off to 3400 psi of EAN36.

Approach:To get to the target pressure of 3400 psi, starting with 1800 psi, diver needs to add 1600 psi of something (e.g., pure O₂ and then air).

To determine how much pure O₂ (100%) to add to the HP100 steel tank, then top off with air to reach EAN36, we use partial pressure blending.

Step 1 – Calculate target O₂ pressure:
Final desired mix = 36% O₂
Final pressure = 3400 psi
⇒ 0.36 × 3400 = 1224 psi O₂

Step 2 – Calculate current O₂ pressure:
Existing mix = 32% O₂ at 1800 psi
⇒ 0.32 × 1800 = 576 psi O₂

For 3400 psi final state, diver needs to add 648 psi of O₂ to the tank.
Some of the O₂ comes from pure O₂, some from air.
Let's compute the amount needed from pure O₂ needes.

Step 3 – Set up blending equation:
If we let X = psi of pure O₂ to add, then 0.21 × (1600 - X) will be the amount of O₂ in psi to be added from air.
Remaining top-off will be with air (21% O₂)
576 + X + 0.21 × (1600 - X) = 1224
576 + X - 0.21X + 0.21 × 1600 = 1224
576 + 0.79X + 0.21 × 1600 = 1224

Step 4 – Solve:
912 + 0.79X = 1224
X ≈ 395 psi of pure O₂

Answer:
Add 395 psi of pure O2, then top off with 1205 psi of air to 3400 psi.
The resulting mix will be EAN36.
