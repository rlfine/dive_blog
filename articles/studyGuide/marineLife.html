The Diving Environment - Marine Life

One of the most interesting aspects of the diving environment is the wide variety of living plants and animals that can be seen. Your diving will be more enjoyable if you know more about the aquatic life you encounter..

Just how deadly are various species of aquatic animals? How toxic or venomous are they?

These are important questions for the modern underwater explorer. The purpose of the marine and aquatic life section is to familiarize the diver with some of the more common hazards aquatic animals pose, informing the diver to minimize anxiety when encountering such, and moderate perpetuating any myths.

Scyphozoans are a class within the phylum Cnidaria, commonly known as “true jellyfish.” They are free-swimming, gelatinous marine animals characterized by a medusa-dominant life cycle and stinging tentacles.

Key Characteristics:

  • Body Form: Bell-shaped medusa is the dominant stage
  • Symmetry: Radial symmetry
  • Stinging Cells: Cnidocytes with nematocysts
  • Nervous System: Simple nerve net (no brain)
  • Movement: Pulsation of bell and ocean current drift
  • Lifestyle: Alternates between polyp and medusa stages

Sting Effects on Divers:

  • Moon Jellyfish (Aurelia aurita): Mild stinging or rash
  • Lion’s Mane Jellyfish (Cyanea capillata): Moderate to severe pain, possible systemic symptoms with large exposures
  • Purple Jellyfish (Pelagia noctiluca): More painful stings, possible dizziness or nausea

Recommended First Aid:

  1. Exit the water safely to avoid further stings
  2. Rinse area with vinegar (5% acetic acid); if unavailable, use saltwater
  3. Remove tentacles with tweezers or gloved hands—do not rub the skin
  4. Immerse the sting site in hot water (104–113°F / 40–45°C) for 20–45 minutes
  5. Monitor for signs of allergic reaction (difficulty breathing, facial swelling)
  6. Apply topical antihistamine or hydrocortisone cream for lingering irritation
  7. Seek medical care if symptoms are severe or persist

Scyphozoan stings are rarely fatal, but proper first aid can prevent complications and reduce pain. Awareness of their characteristics is important for diver safety.

Venomous animals actively inject toxins into another organism through a bite, sting, spine, or barb.
Poisonous animals are toxic when they are touched, ingested, or otherwise passively contacted.

Key Differences:

  • Venomous: Delivers toxin intentionally (e.g., lionfish, box jellyfish, sea snakes)
  • Poisonous: Delivers toxin passively (e.g., pufferfish, some nudibranchs, certain sponges)

Diver Safety Tip:

Never assume a marine animal is safe just because it doesn't sting—poisonous species can be equally dangerous if handled or consumed. Avoid contact with unfamiliar marine life.

The position that each species occupies in its habitat is known as the species’ ecological niche.

Explanation:

An ecological niche describes how a species interacts with both the living (biotic) and nonliving (abiotic) components of its environment. This includes:

  • The resources it uses (e.g., food, shelter)
  • Its functional role in the ecosystem (e.g., predator, scavenger, nitrogen fixer)
  • Its interactions with other organisms (e.g., competition, symbiosis, predation)
  • The specific environmental conditions it requires (e.g., temperature, salinity, depth)

Think of a niche as the species’ “job” or function in the ecosystem, while its habitat is the “address” where it lives.

Bioaccumulation in the context of marine life refers to the gradual build-up of toxic substances—such as heavy metals, pesticides, or naturally occurring toxins like ciguatoxin—in the tissues of an organism over time.

Biomagnification is the process by which the concentration of toxic substances increases at each successive level of a food chain.

🔍Difference from Bioaccumulation:

  • Bioaccumulation happens within a single organism over time.
  • Biomagnification happens across a food chain, with toxins becoming more concentrated in predators at the top.

🌊 Marine Example:

  1. Plankton absorb mercury or ciguatoxin from water.
  2. Small fish eat lots of plankton → toxin concentration increases in their bodies.
  3. Larger fish (e.g., grouper) eat many small fish → more concentrated toxin.
  4. Top predators (e.g., sharks, barracuda, humans) end up with the highest toxin levels.

⚠️ Why It Matters:

  1. Toxins like mercury, PCBs, and ciguatoxins can harm marine life and humans.
  2. Pregnant individuals and children are especially vulnerable to mercury in seafood..
  3. Divers and travelers are often advised to avoid large reef predators in tropical regions.

Scombroid poisoning occurs when individuals eat fish that are partially decomposed, containing high levels of histamine as a result. Bacteria cause the breakdown of the tissue histidine into histamine and saurine at temperatures above 37°C (99°F). The spoiled fish frequently has a peppery or sharp taste, which to some people is not unpleasant..

Always eat fresh fish that has been well refrigerated.

  • Wash the area thoroughly with soap and fresh water. (Tap water is fine.)
  • Remove foreign material.
  • Control bleeding (if present).
  • Pain control: Immerse the affected area in nonscalding fresh water upper limit 45°C (113°F) for 30 to 90 minutes. Repeat as needed.
  • Apply topical antibiotic ointment or cream, if available.
  • Apply bandaging as necessary.
  • Administer pain-control medications, if necessary.
  • Seek professional medical evaluation; medical management may include sedatives, tetanus vaccination and antibiotics. Advanced life support may be required in rare instances.

Note:

The use of heat to the affected area is effective for pain control and can be repeated as needed. Cold packs can also be used and may provide relief and reduce or minimize swelling.

Advanced Concepts

Thermolysis describes the use of heat (often by immersion of the affected area in hot water) to break down substances (thermo meaning heat, and lysis meaning breakdown or destruction). For venoms comprised primarily of proteins, this may denature them and reduce their potency.

Protein denaturation, however, may not necessarily be limited to venom and may also injure healthy tissue in the affected area. Each case is unique and requires some estimation of the depth to which the venom was injected.
