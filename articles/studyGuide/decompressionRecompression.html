As divers transition from rental equipment to their own gear, a solid foundation in dive physics, physiology, and marine awareness ensures informed decisions and safer dives.

Haldane developed the first mathematical decompression model in 1908, proposing that inert gases absorbed under pressure must be released slowly to prevent decompression sickness (DCS). He introduced the concept of tissue compartments and staged decompression.

Tissue compartments are mathematical representations of how fast or slow different body tissues absorb and release inert gases like nitrogen. Each compartment is assigned a half-time for gas uptake and release.

Haldane's model used a maximum allowable supersaturation ratio, where no tissue compartment should exceed twice the ambient pressure when surfacing, i.e., a 2:1 ratio, to avoid bubble formation.

The Haldane model assumes that bubbles don’t form as long as certain pressure ratios aren't exceeded. However, real-world data shows that microbubbles can still form even when these limits are respected, prompting development of newer models.

The U.S. Navy refined the Haldane model using more empirical data from dive experiments. They developed the first Navy dive tables and added compartments with different half-times and more conservative supersaturation limits.

M-values represent the maximum tolerated inert gas pressure in a tissue compartment at a given ambient pressure. They define the upper limit of supersaturation before decompression sickness becomes likely.

Workman developed M-values to enable flexible multilevel dive planning using residual nitrogen levels across different depth profiles rather than simple square dives.

The DCIEM model is based on extensive laboratory and field data, incorporating both perfusion-limited and diffusion-limited gas exchange. It is known for being more conservative, particularly on repetitive and deep dives.

DCIEM tables are frequently used by military and commercial divers in Canada and other countries for air and mixed-gas diving operations due to their conservative safety profile.

DCIEM includes more rigorous safety margins and real-time data from test subjects, especially on repetitive exposures, making it more cautious in managing nitrogen retention.

Dive tables help divers understand the underlying theory of decompression and provide a reliable backup in case of dive computer failure. They promote good dive planning and conservative practices.

A pressure group is a letter code that represents the residual nitrogen level in a diver’s body after a dive. It helps determine the necessary surface interval or adjusted no-decompression time for repetitive dives.

RNT is the amount of nitrogen still present in your body after a dive, expressed in minutes and used to adjust bottom time calculations on subsequent dives.

The no-decompression limit (NDL) for 80 feet is 30 minutes according to NAUI tables.

According to NAUI tables, the diver would surface in pressure group "H" after this dive.

From group "H" with 45 minutes of surface interval, the diver would be in group "D".

At 40 feet, with 15 minutes RNT from group "D", the total bottom time is 50 minutes, which is within the 100-minute NDL for that depth.

A deep stop is a short pause during ascent, usually halfway to the surface from the maximum depth, used to reduce microbubble formation. A safety stop is performed at 15 feet for 3 minutes regardless of depth, as an added safety buffer.

RGBM models penalize reverse profiles (going deeper after a shallower dive) due to increased bubble formation risk, which standard models may not fully account for.

RGBM uses bubble growth control and limits based on prior dive histories to extend surface interval requirements and reduce the number of dives per day.

DAN recommends a minimum wait of 12 hours after a single no-decompression dive, 18 hours after multiple dives or multiple days of diving, and even longer after decompression dives.

Dive computers calculate real-time decompression status based on actual dive profiles, allowing for more flexible and precise nitrogen tracking than standard dive tables.

Different brands use variations of Bühlmann, RGBM, or proprietary algorithms. These vary in conservatism, especially on repetitive and multiday dives, and divers should understand their computer's model and safety factors.

PPO2 (partial pressure of oxygen) indicates how much oxygen is being absorbed at depth. A PPO2 above 1.4 ATA during the working portion of a dive increases the risk of oxygen toxicity.

Nitrox has less nitrogen than air, which lowers nitrogen absorption per dive, reducing the risk of DCS, especially for repetitive dives with short surface intervals.

The MOD for EANx32 at a PPO2 limit of 1.4 ATA is 111 feet. Exceeding this depth increases the risk of central nervous system oxygen toxicity.

PPO2 = FO2 × ATA. For example, diving to 99 feet (4 ATA) on EANx36 (0.36 O2) results in a PPO2 of 1.44 ATA, exceeding the recommended working limit of 1.4.

Exceeding 1.6 ATA increases the risk of convulsions and blackout due to oxygen toxicity, which can be fatal underwater.

Administer 100% oxygen immediately, keep the diver horizontal and calm, monitor vital signs, and transport them to a hyperbaric facility without delay.

Hyperbaric oxygen therapy re-compresses the diver and flushes nitrogen from tissues under high-pressure oxygen, aiding in symptom reversal and tissue recovery.

Besides oxygen and recompression therapy, hydration, warmth, horizontal positioning, and prompt medical evaluation are critical in mitigating decompression injuries.

Use the formula MOD (ft) = [(PPO₂ limit / FO₂) - 1] × 33. For EANx32 and a PPO₂ of 1.4: [(1.4 / 0.32) - 1] × 33 = 111 feet.

Use the formula MOD (m) = [(PPO₂ limit / FO₂) - 1] × 10. For EANx32 and a PPO₂ of 1.4: [(1.4 / 0.32) - 1] × 10 = 24.75 meters.

NDL is the maximum time a diver can stay at depth without mandatory decompression stops. A decompression ceiling is the shallowest depth a diver can ascend to without violating required decompression obligations, common in technical diving profiles.

Microbubbles are tiny inert gas bubbles that form in tissues during ascent. While often asymptomatic, their accumulation can lead to DCS. Models like RGBM aim to minimize these through slower ascents and deep stops.

Dehydration reduces blood volume and impairs tissue perfusion, slowing inert gas elimination and increasing DCS risk. Divers should hydrate before and after dives, especially in warm climates or during multiple dive days.

Fat stores absorb more nitrogen, and older individuals may have reduced circulation and slower off-gassing. These factors can increase DCS risk during repetitive or deep dives.

Cold causes peripheral vasoconstriction, directing blood to the core. This limits nitrogen uptake in limbs but increases it in central tissues. Cold also slows off-gassing, making conservative profiles essential.

Multi-level dives reduce nitrogen loading by ascending gradually and spending less time at maximum depth. However, without a computer or proper planning, they can lead to underestimation of accumulated nitrogen.

Use conservative dive profiles, extend surface intervals, track residual nitrogen and CNS oxygen exposure (for Nitrox), and remain hydrated.

Plan deeper dives first, followed by shallower ones later in the day or trip.

A simulator allows divers to preview profiles and visualize nitrogen loading and ascent behavior. It’s a great tool for training, risk assessment, and understanding decompression theory in real time.

NAUI tables tend to offer longer surface interval credits than PADI. RGBM uses bubble dynamics, often requiring longer intervals and penalizing fast repetitive dives. Understanding your model’s assumptions is key.

Early signs include joint/muscle pain, fatigue, tingling, dizziness, itching, and skin rash. More severe symptoms involve numbness, weakness, or confusion. Any symptom post-dive should prompt oxygen administration and medical evaluation.

At altitude, the ambient pressure is lower, reducing pressure gradients and affecting nitrogen off-gassing. Divers must use altitude-adjusted tables or computers capable of calculating at elevation to avoid DCS.

Physical activity increases circulation, potentially transporting gas bubbles to sensitive tissues. Light activity may help off-gassing, but heavy exertion too soon post-dive increases DCS risk.

Breathing pure oxygen between dives accelerates nitrogen elimination by increasing the pressure gradient and displacing nitrogen in the lungs. It's sometimes used in technical diving surface protocols.

The CNS clock tracks cumulative oxygen exposure based on PPO2 and time. A 100% CNS exposure (based on NOAA limits) is considered the safe daily maximum. Dive computers or manual tables can be used to track it.

1.6 ATA is the emergency maximum. It’s acceptable for short exposures during decompression stops, but not recommended for working portions of dives due to increased seizure risk.

Planned dives should stay below 1.4 ATA PPO2.
