Are You a Good Dive Buddy?

Taking the Responsibility of Being a Dive Buddy Seriously

“Plan your dive. Dive your plan.”
But don’t forget: You are part of someone else’s plan.

Too often on dive boats, especially in popular destinations, divers are paired with little more than a name exchange and a glance at each other’s gear. One diver might be a photographer eager to chase the perfect shot inside a coral head. The other might be on their first drift dive, nervously trying to keep up. Both may assume the other knows what they’re doing. Neither has a shared understanding of the plan—or of each other.

The result? Dive buddies that surface 30 meters apart, unaware of each other's status, incapable of providing immediate aid if something goes wrong underwater. That’s not just poor form—it’s dangerous.

Being a dive buddy isn't a checkbox on the dive roster—it's an active role that can make the difference between a smooth dive and a serious emergency. Good buddy diving requires preparation, communication, awareness, and mutual support.

This post is about what it means to be a responsible dive buddy, and how to take that role seriously every time you enter the water.

1. Pre-Dive: The Conversation You Must Have

Before the “giant stride,” take a moment for real communication, especially with a newly assigned buddy. The pre-dive chat should cover:

  • Gear Familiarity: Do you know how to release your buddy’s weights? Are their releases buckles, Velcro, or pull handles? Is their alternate air source integrated into the BCD or a standard octopus?
  • Dive Objectives: Are you both here to explore or is one taking photos or doing a skills dive? Any personal limits (depth, air, deco)?
  • Experience Levels: Who has more drift dive experience? Night dive? Wreck penetration? Any past incidents or medical issues the other should be aware of?
  • Emergency Expectations: At what air pressure will you turn the dive? What’s the maximum agreed-upon separation distance? Do you both carry a signaling device (DSMB, whistle, light)?
  • Final Check: Use your training agency’s checklist like BWRAF or SEABAG. It’s not just for students.
2. During the Dive: Active Awareness

This is where buddy responsibility becomes most visible—and most often neglected.

  • Maintain Visual or Physical Contact: In low viz or drift currents, physical contact may be necessary. In clear water, visual contact must be frequent—not occasional.
  • Shared Situational Awareness: Regular air checks: “I’m at 1900. You?” Constant orientation: "We’ve drifted 50m from the reef wall." Depth matching: Avoid vertical separation—it kills visibility and awareness.
3. Adjusting for Each Other

If one diver gets fascinated by a spotted eel, the other doesn’t wander off. If one wants to hang back to examine coral, the buddy stays close. If you’re bored—too bad. Your job is to be present.

Positioning matters. If you’re 1 meter behind and 1 meter above your buddy, you might have excellent visibility and awareness of them—but you’re in their blind spot.
To you, everything’s fine.
To them, you’re gone.

Make it a habit to stay within your buddy’s field of view, not just in range of your own.

4. After the Dive: Debrief Together

Being a good buddy continues after the dive. Discuss:

  • Was anything unclear or unexpected?
  • Did you lose visual contact? Why?
  • Did either of you feel unsafe or unsure at any point?

Use this to improve your next dive together—or to respectfully speak up if the pairing needs to change.

Final Thoughts: The Buddy You’d Want

Being a good dive buddy isn’t just about checking boxes—it’s about being reliable, predictable, and aware.

If something went wrong on your dive…
Would you want you as your buddy?

Take your responsibility seriously. Dive safe. And be the kind of diver others trust without question.
