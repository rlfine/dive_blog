Boyle’s Law (circa 1662)pressure–volume

Definition & equation
Isothermal: P₁·V₁ = P₂·V₂ (same gas, constant temperature, fixed moles)

Plain English: For a fixed amount of gas at constant temperature, pressure and volume change inversely.

Diver example

Mask air space 150 mL at 10 m/33 ft (~2 ATA) becomes ~300 mL at the surface (~1 ATA). Vent through the nose on ascent to avoid mask squeeze and over‑pressure.

Common pitfalls
  • Using gauge instead of absolute pressure. Always use ATA/bar absolute.
  • Forgetting temperature changes (rapid gas expansion can cool).

Gay‑Lussac’s Law (circa 1862)pressure–temperature

Definition & equation
Isochoric: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (constant volume; T in Kelvin)

At fixed volume, pressure is proportional to absolute temperature.

Diver example (hot car)

A cylinder at 200 bar at 20 °C (293 K) warms to 50 °C (323 K): P₂ ≈ 200·(323/293) ≈ 220.5 bar.

Common pitfalls
  • Reading a hot‑fill as “full” — it drops after cooling.
  • Using °C/°F directly instead of K in the ratio.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures (circa 1180287)gas fractions

Definition & equations
Ptotal = ΣPi,   Pi = Fi·Ptotal

Each gas in a mixture contributes a partial pressure equal to its fraction times the total pressure.

Diver example (EAN32 at 30 m/100 ft)

Total pressure ≈ 4 ATA → PPO₂ = 0.32·4 = 1.28 ATA, PPN₂ = 0.68·4 = 2.72 ATA.

Common pitfalls
  • Confusing fraction with percent (use 0.32, not 32).
  • Ignoring water vapor in closed systems (minor in open circuit at depth).

Charles’s Law (circa 1787)volume–temperature

Definition & equation
Isobaric: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant pressure; T in Kelvin)

Volume changes directly with absolute temperature when pressure is constant (e.g., flexible bags).

Diver example

A 10 L DSMB left in the sun warms from 20 °C (293 K) to 35 °C (308 K) → V₂ ≈ 10·(308/293) ≈ 10.5 L.

Henry’s Law (circa 1803)dissolved gas

Definition & equation
C = kH · Pgas (at given temperature)

Gas dissolved in a liquid is proportional to that gas’s partial pressure.

Diver relevance
  • Greater PPN₂ at depth → more N₂ dissolves in tissues.
  • On ascent, PPN₂ falls → off‑gassing; too steep a gradient risks bubbles.
  • Colder water ↑ solubility; perfusion/exertion modifies uptake/elimination.

Archimedes’ Principle (circa 250 BCE)buoyancy

Definition
Buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid.

Each 1 L of extra gas volume adds ≈1 kgf (~2.2 lbf) lift in freshwater; slightly more in seawater.

Diver takeaways
  • Wetsuit compression on descent → you become more negative; add BC gas.
  • Gas expansion on ascent → vent early/often to prevent runaway buoyancy.

Assumptions & Practical Limits

Absolute units

  • Pressure: ATA or bar absolute (surface ≈ 1 ATA).
  • Temperature: Kelvin (K) for gas‑law ratios: T(K)=T(°C)+273.15.

Same gas, fixed moles

  • Boyle/Charles/Gay‑Lussac assume no gas added/removed and the same sample.
  • Dalton uses fractions; Henry ties partial pressure to dissolved amount.

Real‑world caveats

  • Non‑ideal behavior at high P/low T (compressibility, humidity).
  • Fast fills heat tanks; readings drift as they cool.
  • Physiology (perfusion, temperature, exercise) modifies Henry‑law dynamics.

Practice Problems (with reveal answers)

1) Boyle — Bubble expansion on ascent

A 0.8 mL bubble at 20 m (~3 ATA) rises to 10 m (~2 ATA). What volume at 10 m? Assume constant T.

Answer

V₂ = V₁·(P₁/P₂) = 0.8·(3/2) = 1.2 mL.

2) Gay‑Lussac — Cold quarry morning

Tank shows 3000 psi at 86 °F (≈303 K). What pressure at 50 °F (≈283 K)?

Answer

P₂ = 3000·(283/303) ≈ 2800 psi (≈ −7%).

3) Dalton — MOD check for EAN36

Working PPO₂ limit 1.4 ATA. What’s the MOD for EAN36?

Answer

MOD = (1.4/0.36 − 1)×33 ≈ 95 ft (≈28.9 m).

4) Henry — Off‑gassing concept

Why do we ascend slowly and make shallow stops?

Answer

Reducing ambient pressure gradually keeps tissue supersaturation within allowable gradients so dissolved inert gas can leave solution without forming problematic bubbles.

5) Archimedes — Lung buoyancy tweak

At a shallow safety stop in freshwater, you inhale ~0.6 L. How much lift change?

Answer

≈0.6 kgf (≈1.3 lbf) additional buoyancy.
