Simulating a BCD Failure at 120 Feet: A Controlled Dive Challenge, Part 2

In Part 1 of this series on handling the unexpected (a BCD failure at depth), we talked in general terms on how to approach a simulated emergency situation. We stressed actual emergency training as a means of taking fear and panic out of the equation by approaching the emergency situation with calm and confidence.

In this posting, we look deeper into the many aspects of a specific case, and attempt to show that by keeping the mind busy using the stop, think, act approach, we can push panic to the back seat and give the diver time to focus quickly but calmly on addressing the requisite recovery steps.

Introduction: Why Train for BCD Failure and How?

For most recreational divers, a buoyancy control device (BCD) is a trusted and reliable piece of gear. But what happens if it fails at depth? Preparing for unlikely—yet possible—equipment failures is a hallmark of advanced diver training. This post explores a controlled simulation of a BCD failure at 120 feet, examining the physics, planning, and safety protocols that make such training valuable and effective.

The Dive Scenario: 120 Feet, Steel Tank, 5mm Wetsuit

The simulation involves descending to 120 feet with a 5mm wetsuit and a steel HP100 tank, starting with 1800 PSI of air. For this simulation exercise, the diver chooses to not ditch weights and rely only breath control and finning to ascend, stopping at 50 feet for a brief pause, then at 15 feet for a full 3-minute safety stop.

Important: This simulation, as with all emergency simulation traing, is to be performed under the direct supervision of an experienced instructor with redundant gas and buoyancy systems in place. Do not attempt this without proper training, preparation, and supervision.

Buoyancy at Depth: Physics and Practicalities

At 120 feet, the 5mm wetsuit is highly compressed, offering minimal buoyancy. The steel tank is inherently negative, and with 1800 PSI (~52 cubic feet of gas), the diver is negatively buoyant overall (potentially significantly negatively buoyant, depending on the amount of lead needed to compensate for the buoyancy of the 5mm wetsuit and full tank at the surface at the beginning of the dive).

As the diver ascends, the wetsuit re-expands, increasing buoyancy—especially pronounced above 30 feet, where careful breath control becomes crucial to prevent an uncontrolled ascent.

Quick Math: The available gas was calculated as (1800 PSI / 3442 PSI) × 100 cf = ~52.3 cf (~1479 liters).

Gas Planning and Air Use: Crunching the Numbers

At 120 feet (~5.6 ATA), air consumption multiplies rapidly. With a moderate SAC rate of 0.8 cf/min, we can estimate the diver using approximately:

  • ~11.3 cf ascending from 120 ft to 50 ft (2.5 min)
  • ~2.0 cf at 50 ft pause (1 min)
  • ~2.4 cf ascending to 15 ft (1.2 min)
  • ~3.6 cf during 3-minute safety stop
  • ~1.2 cf ascending to the surface

Total estimated use: ~20.5 cf, leaving a solid safety buffer.

Safety First: Why Supervision and Backup Are Non-Negotiable

Though this is a simulation, the diver’s BCD remains functional and could be inflated if needed. Redundant air and a vigilant instructor are essential components of the plan. Realistic failure training is valuable only when paired with robust safety nets and a clear abort strategy.

Safety Reminder: Never attempt buoyancy failure simulations solo or without direct instructor oversight. The line between controlled challenge and life-threatening emergency is razor-thin.

Personal Insights: Building Confidence Through Challenge

This experience highlights the importance of composure, precise finning, and lung control. It underscores the need for thoughtful dive planning and reaffirms that true mastery involves understanding both your equipment—and your limits. Training like this serves to build the muscle memory and confidence to handle real-world failures should they occur.

Actionable Takeaways: How to Plan Your Own Simulation (Safely)

  • Train progressively: Start at 30–60 feet before attempting deep drills.
  • Always brief your instructor and dive team on your plan.
  • Carry redundant air and buoyancy systems.
  • Set clear abort criteria (e.g., rising SAC rate, fatigue).
  • Debrief thoroughly and log your air use and personal performance.
