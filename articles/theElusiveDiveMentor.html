What Happens to Wetsuit Buoyancy at Depth? A Closer Look for Curious Divers

During a recent PADI course, after our second dive, I posed a question that I thought would make for a good mental exercise in dive physics:

“If a diver is perfectly weighted at the surface in a 7mm wetsuit—let’s say needing 21 pounds of lead to be neutrally buoyant with a lung filled breath of air—how much negatively buoyant support would the diver need to be at 40 meters, where Boyle’s Law predicts significant neoprene compression?
Would the 21 lbs of lead needed at the surface be just the right amount at 40 meters, would it be just a bit too much, or wuld it be a lot too much”

I have now posed this question to 3 separate PADI dive instructors. The answers I received were... interesting.

  • One instructor brushed it aside entirely, noting that he always dives a drysuit—implying that when diving cold water, drysuit diving was the only serious diving worth discussing.
  • Another instructor offered an explanation that lift is never affected by depth because the air in your BCD is always at ambient pressure (which is technically true, but misses the point of volume-based buoyancy).
  • A third shifted the conversation to his experience diving in a 3mm suit in warm, salt water—which is a very different scenario altogether.

While I appreciate each of these instructors' backgrounds and experience, none directly addressed the original physics-based question.

I am still looking for a definitive and convincing response to the question I posed many blogs posts ago (in reference to a Texas lake dive fataility of a diver in a 7mmm Wetsuit):

Is deep diving in cold water, in a 7mm wetsuit safe.

And maybe that should serve as a valuable reminder:

Divers should seek out instructors not just for their dive experience, but for their ability to explain the part of diving you're trying to master.

An instructor who’s great at introducing first-time divers to open water might not be the one you turn to for deep diving physics or buoyancy management in technical environments.

So What Really Happens to a Wetsuit at Depth?

Neoprene wetsuits provide lift at the surface due to tiny gas bubbles in the foam. As depth increases, pressure compresses those bubbles, reducing volume—and thus reducing the wetsuit’s ability to displace water. That means you lose buoyant lift as you descend.

According to Boyle’s Law:

At constant temperature, the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to the surrounding pressure:
V₂ = V₁ × (P₁ / P₂)

At 40 meters (or 130 feet), the pressure is roughly 5 ATA. That means air spaces shrink to about 1/5 of their surface volume—including the air trapped in neoprene. So a 7mm wetsuit that provides +21 pounds of lift at the surface now offers only about +4.2 pounds of lift at depth. That's assuming all of the lift in the wetsuit is due to trapped gas chambers - and for the most part that is true. It is certainly good enough for our back of the envelope analysis. We will jump in the lake some day soon and test the hypothesis, and update here based on recorded emperical data.

For now, we assume our 7mm wetsuite has near same characteristics of a ballon with enough air volume at the surface to generate +21 lbs of lift.

Chart: Buoyancy Loss in a 7mm Wetsuit by Depth

Depth (meters) Ambient Pressure (ATA) Buoyancy (lbs)
0 m (Surface) 1 ATA +21 lbs
10 m 2 ATA +10.5 lbs
20 m 3 ATA +7 lbs
30 m 4 ATA +5.25 lbs
40 m 5 ATA +4.2 lbs

What This Means for You

If you were neutrally buoyant at the surface with 21 lbs of ballast, and your wetsuit loses 80% of its buoyant lift by 130 ft, then you’ll be nearly 17 lbs negatively buoyant unless you add air to your BCD. So yes—you will need to add buoyancy during descent to maintain control, even if you start the dive perfectly trimmed.

The key takeaway isn’t to criticize instructors—but to remind divers to remain curious and keep learning. Ask questions. Shop around for instructors who bring clarity and precision to the topics you care about most. The best mentor for your first dive may not be the best one for your next specialty course.

Bottom Line: If your BCD fails at 40 meters with a 7mm wetsuit and 21 lbs of lead, you better have a backup plan.

What Makes a Great Instructor?

While dive experience matters, it's not the only metric that makes an instructor valuable. A truly effective instructor should be able to operate in the abstract—applying known facts to new or hypothetical situations. In other words:

A good instructor doesn’t need to have personally experienced wearing a 7mm wetsuit to 40 meters and suddenly realizing that the BCD failed in the most catostrophic way to explain what happens. No one would reasonable expect an instructo to have personally experienced every imaginable conbination of events. But we do have a very well grounded set of scuba diving gas and pressure theories, and a good instructor should be capable of combining and explaining how they might apply in a real word situation. They should be able to combine Boyle’s Law, neoprene compression behavior, and pressure at depth to reason through the outcome.

That’s the mark of a skilled mentor—not someone who just tells you what worked for them, but someone who can help you understand why it worked, and how it might change under different conditions.
