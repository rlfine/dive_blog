Vacuum, Pressure & Gas Mixtures
A Diver’s Guide
NAUI Master Scuba Diver Theory

A web presentation about what pressure really is, why “vacuum” isn’t suction, how Dalton and the Ideal Gas Law fit together, and why this matters to divers.

1) The everyday myth: “vacuum sucks”
Myth vs. Reality

There’s no such thing as negative pressure.

People say a vacuum “sucks,” as if it pulls air in. In physics, pressure is either absolute (always ≥ 0) or measured relative to ambient (gauge). What we call “suction” is simply higher pressure pushing toward a lower pressure region.

Key idea: Air moves from higher pressure to lower pressure because gas molecules already carry kinetic energy; nothing “reaches in” to pull them.
2) What pressure is (absolute vs gauge)

Pressure is force per area: P = F / A

Absolute vs Gauge

  • PSIA / bar(a): measured from a true vacuum (0 absolute).
  • PSIG / bar(g): measured relative to ambient. 0 psig ≈ 14.7 psia at sea level.

Useful anchors

  • 1 atm ≈ 14.7 psi ≈ 1.01325 bar.
  • Sea level ambient ≈ 1 atm (absolute).
  • Dive pressure increases ≈ 1 atm every ~10 m seawater (~33 ft).

Note: “STP” sometimes means 1 atm and 0 °C (molar volume ≈ 22.414 L/mol). Some standards use 1 bar (22.711 L/mol). State which convention you use.

3) What a vacuum really means

“Vacuum” = fewer molecules than ambient

A total vacuum is a perfect absence of matter (never achieved). A partial vacuum is simply a region with lower absolute pressure than its surroundings — i.e., fewer gas molecules per unit volume.

Language tip for divers: “Negative pressure test” really means “below ambient” (gauge-negative), not pressure < 0 absolute.
4) Kinetic theory: why gases push

Pressure comes from molecular collisions with surfaces

Gas molecules incessantly move and collide with container walls. The collective momentum transfer per area per time is what we measure as pressure. More molecules (or higher temperature) → more collisions → higher pressure.

At fixed volume and temperature:   P ∝ N (number of molecules)
5) What pumps actually do

Pumps create a lower-pressure path, molecules do the rest

A pump doesn’t “grab” gas inside your vessel. It reduces pressure on the outlet side, so the natural random motion of molecules favors escape toward the lower pressure region. Over many collisions, more go out than return until a new equilibrium is reached.

Two broad families (plain-English)

  • Positive-displacement (e.g., piston/rotary vane): enlarges volume to reduce pressure and move gas out.
  • Molecular/entrainment (e.g., turbomolecular, diffusion): transfer momentum to molecules, sweeping them away.
Diver analogy: Bubbles don’t “get pulled up”; pressure is greater below and lower above, so buoyant forces push them upward.
6) Why a perfect vacuum is unreachable

The “last molecule” paradox

To remove the very last molecule from a container, the outside would have to be at an even lower pressure. But then to empty the outside, you’d need an even “emptier” region, and so on. Practically, pumps approach lower and lower pressures asymptotically but never reach zero molecules.

Reality check: Ultra-high vacuum is possible; absolute zero pressure is not.
7) Dalton + Ideal Gas Law

Dalton’s Law (mixtures)

Ptotal = Σ Pi   and   Pi = xi · Ptotal

Each gas contributes a partial pressure proportional to its mole fraction xi.

Ideal Gas Law

P·V = n·R·T

At fixed V and T, pressure scales with the amount of gas: P ∝ n (or ∝ molecule count N).

These two ideas dovetail: if you halve the number of molecules of each component, each partial pressure halves, and the total halves.

8) Worked examples (diver-relevant)

Assume STP = 1 atm and 0 °C (22.414 L/mol). Vessel volume = 1 L.

8.1 · How many molecules are in 1 L at STP?

  • Moles in 1 L: n = 1 / 22.414 ≈ 0.044615 mol
  • Avogadro’s number: NA = 6.02214076×10²³ mol⁻¹
  • Total molecules: N ≈ 0.044615 × NA ≈ 2.6868×10²²
80/20 mixture → N₂ molecules ≈ 2.149×10²², O₂ molecules ≈ 5.374×10²¹.

8.2 · Remove half the molecules (same T, same V). What is the pressure?

By PV = nRT, halving n halves P. Starting from 1 atm (≈14.7 psi), new pressure ≈ 0.5 atm (7.35 psi). Each partial pressure halves as well (Dalton).

8.3 · Extreme thought experiment: only 10 molecules remain (8 N₂ + 2 O₂)

Pressure scales with molecule count (at fixed T, V):

Pnew = PSTP × (Nnew / NSTP) = 14.7 psi × (10 / 2.6868×10²²) ≈ 5.47×10−21 psi

That’s effectively a near-vacuum; at so few molecules, pressure becomes a statistical (not smooth) quantity.

8.4 · The opposite: Pack 1 mole of gas into the same 1 L at 0 °C

By the Ideal Gas Law: P = nRT/V = (1 mol)(0.082057 L·atm/mol·K)(273.15 K)/1 L ≈ 22.414 atm → ≈ 329.4 psi.

Component Mole fraction Partial pressure
N₂ 0.80 ≈ 17.93 atm ≈ 263.5 psi
O₂ 0.20 ≈ 4.48 atm ≈ 65.9 psi
Total 1.00 ≈ 22.41 atm ≈ 329.4 psi

If you prefer the 1 bar STP convention (22.711 L/mol), the molecule counts and scaling change slightly, but the logic is identical. Always state your STP convention.

9) Diver applications & takeaways

Tank “empty” ≠ no molecules

At 0 psig your cylinder still has ~14.7 psia inside:

  • trillions upon trillions of molecules remain.
  • Empty means equal to ambient, not vacuum.

Equalization language

When we say “negative pressure” across the eardrum or under a mask, we mean below ambient (gauge-negative), not pressure < 0 absolute.

Dalton’s in gas planning

ppO₂ and ppN₂ track with fractions × depth pressure. The physiology (e.g., O₂ toxicity limits, N₂ narcosis) keys off partial pressures, not just mix labels.

Henry’s Law is different

Henry’s Law relates dissolved gas in tissues to partial pressure in breathing gas. Our vacuum/pump discussion is Dalton + Ideal Gas Law, not Henry.

Bottom line:
  • Pressure is about how many molecules (and how fast) hit surfaces.
  • To reduce the pressure in a fixed geometric volume:
    • lower the kinetic energy by lowering the temperature or
    • reduce the number of molecules
  • Pumps don’t “suck”; they create a lower-pressure path.
  • Dalton explains mixture shares; the Ideal Gas Law explains scaling.

Sidebar · Cylinder size vs. free gas volume

A standard 11-liter scuba cylinder has a fixed internal volume of about 11 L — this is the cylinder volume or water capacity. When filled to 200 bar, the gas inside is compressed ~200× compared to surface pressure.

That means the cylinder actually contains the same number of molecules as about 2200 liters of free air at 1 bar. This larger number is called the free gas volume, and it represents the breathing gas you could expand and use at the surface.

Summary:
• Cylinder volume = physical container size (11 L).
• Free gas volume = usable air if expanded to 1 bar (~2000–2200 L).
