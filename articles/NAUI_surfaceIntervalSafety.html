Surface Intervals Less Than 10 Minutes in NAUI Air Dive Tables

Recreational dive tables, including those published by NAUI, do not credit surface intervals of less than 10 minutes when performing repetitive dive calculations. But why? And how does this relate to other important safety concepts like safety stops, tissue compartment behavior, and the risk of decompression sickness (DCS) over multiple dives?

This article walks through the detailed reasoning behind short surface intervals, the purpose of safety stops, and the differences between fast and slow tissue compartments in decompression theory.

Whether you're a dive instructor or an aspiring Master Scuba Diver, this deep dive will help you anticipate the tough questions your students might ask — and help you answer them with confidence.

Why Surface Intervals < 10 Minutes Don’t Count

In NAUI Air Dive Tables, a surface interval of less than 10 minutes is not credited because it is not long enough to permit significant nitrogen off-gassing, especially from the slower tissue compartments. In fact, if we look cloely at the NAUI AIR DIVE TABLE, we see the mapping from the surface interval to the dive letter group for the next dive has no entries for less than 10 minutes. For example, Group G has 7 entries ranging from 24:00-5:42 all the way down to 0:50-0:10. What letter group do we use for the next dive after a 5 minute surface interval following a 35 minute dive to 60 feet? Here's the rule, and a bit leter we will develop the theory:

If diver surfaces and descends again within the window 0 to 10 minute, the two dives are treated as one continuous multilevel exposure. This is a conservative assumption that prevents underestimating residual nitrogen.
  • Rule: If SI < 10 minutes → treat as a single dive
  • Combine total bottom time
  • Use the deepest depth reached

That sounds a whole lot like the NAUI dive tables are discounting the efficacy of a 10 minute surface interval. If that is the case, then we are open to asking the next really important question - If the 10 minute SI is not counted, then why is there such a focus on the 3 minute safety stop at 12 feet? Why Safety Stops Are Still Valuable

A 3–5 minute safety stop at 5 meters (15 feet) is not a surface interval, nor is it credited in dive table planning. However, it significantly reduces decompression risk by allowing fast tissues to off-gas more gently, reducing microbubble formation.

Fast tissues (5–10 min half-times) are more likely to create microbubbles upon ascent if not given time to stabilize. Safety stops serve as a buffer zone to control off-gassing gradients and reduce embolic risk.

Fast vs. Slow Tissues: Off-Gassing and Risk

Fast Tissues Slow Tissues
Half-Time 5–10 min 60–120+ min
Absorption Quick Slow
Off-Gassing Quick Very slow
Bubble Risk High if uncontrolled ascent Low unless multi-dive buildup
Tolerable Tension Low High

Slow tissues tolerate more supersaturation because they absorb and release nitrogen gradually. Fast tissues, by contrast, have lower tolerance and drive ascent profile design.

After Surfacing: Who’s Still Off-Gassing?

Upon surfacing, fast tissues are actively off-gassing and continue to do so for several minutes. If the diver made a safe ascent, the risk of microbubble formation is low. Slow tissues, however, may still be at peak saturation, or even continue to on-gas from venous return flow.

This slow off-gassing becomes critical during repetitive dives. Multiple dives over 3–6 hours can load slow compartments beyond safe levels, particularly if short surface intervals and cold water reduce off-gassing rates.

DCIEM and Bubble Theory

DCIEM, VPM, and other bubble-based models (as opposed to dissolved gas-only models like Bühlmann ZHL) explicitly consider post-dive venous gas emboli. These models highlight that even if fast compartments are “clean,” nitrogen can still be released from slow tissues into circulation and create latent decompression stress.

Thus, repetitive dives that appear conservative can still accumulate risk via slow tissue nitrogen loading and reverse gradients.

Final Takeaway

Surface intervals less than 10 minutes are treated conservatively because they provide negligible relief to the body’s slow tissues. Safety stops, while not counted in repetitive dive planning, are highly effective in reducing microbubble formation in fast tissues. Both practices reflect different safety priorities grounded in physiological reality. A dive leader or instructor should understand these distinctions to answer student questions with clarity and scientific accuracy.
