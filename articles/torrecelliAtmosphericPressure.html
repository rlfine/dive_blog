Torricelli, Torr, and Atmospheric Pressure
A 400-Year-Old Insight

Exploring the science and safety of scuba diving.

A deep dive into pressure, density
and the origin of the unit “torr.”

We often think of ourselves as enlightened and far more learned than our historical predecessors. But the brilliance of 17th-century thinkers like Evangelista Torricelli reminds us that great leaps in understanding were made long before modern instruments.

Yes — but with nuance. Atmospheric pressure, which results from the weight of the air above us, is what holds up the mercury column. However, it's more accurate to say that the mercury column is supported by atmospheric pressure, not direct weight. The "weight of air" explains where pressure originates, but it’s the pressure that balances the column.

While a larger dish does increase the total force exerted by atmospheric pressure (F = P × A), it is the pressure at the point where the mercury enters the tube that matters. Yes. Torricelli would still observe the same column height if the dish were only just large enough to maintain a mercury seal at the tube opening. What matters is that there’s a continuous mercury path exposed to atmospheric pressure — not the overall size of the reservoir.
The mercury column would be shorter. The equation \( h = \frac{P}{\rho g} \) shows that height is inversely related to fluid density. So, a denser liquid like Lead or Bismuth would require a shorter column to balance atmospheric pressure. A hypothetical liquid Nobelium would make the vacuum column even shorter — though Nobelium's instability makes that purely theoretical.
Then the vacuum column would be much taller. For example, to match atmospheric pressure with water, you'd need a column over 10 meters (about 34 feet) tall. This is why mercury — being dense and relatively stable — was ideal. Using a less dense liquid simply stretches the required height.
Torricelli’s experiment was the first proof that air has weight and pressure. It led to the creation of the unit “torr” — 1 torr equals 1 mm Hg. Understanding pressure in terms of weight per unit area helps divers grasp how pressure affects gas volumes, nitrogen absorption, and ascent safety. It’s a timeless reminder: simple physical experiments still teach powerful lessons.
