On the Edge of Knowing: A Reflection on Limits, Discovery, and Humility

Editor's Note:
While this essay steps a bit beyond the usual topics of buoyancy and bottom time, I believe it belongs here. Diving has always been about boundaries — the edge of breath, the edge of visibility, the edge of knowing. This reflection explores those edges from a different angle, one that honors curiosity, acknowledges our limitations, and celebrates the quiet power of asking what if.
— r. fine

Understaning our limits - in practice and in thought

Throughout history, when humanity has declared, "We cannot do that," it often turns out to be a provisional truth rather than an absolute one. Time and again, those declarations have been overturned by the curious, the relentless, and the quietly skeptical. We build what was once unbuildable. We measure the previously immeasurable. We reshape our view of what is possible not through magic or hubris, but through method, perseverance, and wonder.

With all of that, I am still not 100% convinced that all things are possible. To assume so would be a kind of arrogance. We are not that clever. Our reach exceeds our grasp far less often than we pretend - it's out there, we know it is, we just can't get our hands on it no matter the effort applied.

Even the greatest minds are constrained by the limits of biology, of cognition, of finite time. There are questions we may never be able to ask correctly, let alone answer. The boundary between our capabilities and the true nature of reality is not merely a gap in tools or knowledge, but sometimes a wall of principle.

Still, I take issue with the flat finality that sometimes accompanies the phrase, "We cannot." Too often, it becomes a stand-in for "We do not yet know how" or "We have no present method." It really means We/I cannot do it today. It is a snapshot of our limits, not a portrait of the limits of nature. And history is thick with examples where that snapshot aged poorly. If a problem has no acceptable solution today, it may be due to a limit of nature, or it may be due to our own limits. Unfortunately, it is not often that nature tells us which are are our limits and which are hers.

Eschew Cliches

But neither do I hide behind the phrase, "We don't know what we don't know." I confess I do not really understand what it means. It feels too much like a placeholder for awe or mystery, and not always a helpful one. Instead, I find more clarity in this: we often learn something valuable while searching for something else. Penicillin, x-rays, cosmic background radiation, and even the peculiar behavior of quantum systems were not the fruits of precise aims, but the consequence of a mind tuned to curiosity and aware of anomaly. Discovery is rarely linear.

The complacent mind accepts the world as it is. The curious mind studies what is, tests its boundaries, and accidentally steps beyond them. This, to me, is where the deepest value of research lies: not always in solving the intended problem, but in lighting the periphery. Solid, rigorous investigation plants seeds we did not know we were sowing.

So no, not everything is possible. But we should be slow to declare what isn't. Between what we know and what we may come to know is a terrain best navigated with precision, skepticism, and enduring humility.

Avoid the trap of   The Anchoring Bias

To illustrate this tension between theory and practice, consider the string-theoretic notion that everything — a rock, an apple, a person — is ultimately composed of vibrating one-dimensional strings. In this framework, matter is not defined by what it is made of, but by how its most fundamental constituents vibrate. Theoretically, if we could re-tune the vibrational modes of every string in a rock — altering nothing but the waveform — we could transform that rock into an apple. The idea is breathtakingly elegant: matter becomes a question of music, of pattern. Change the symphony, and you change the substance.

Of course, in practice, this is beyond comprehension. We cannot even come close to measuring, let alone manipulating, individual strings. But the theory shows us what might be possible in principle, which is often the first step in learning what could someday be possible in fact. All we can say with confidence is that such transformation exceeds our capability today. It may forever remain so.

But we should never confuse our limitations with nature's rules:

  • To say "never" is to speak for the universe.
  • To say "not yet" is to speak honestly for ourselves.
