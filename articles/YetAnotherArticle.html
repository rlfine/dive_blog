In discussing this scenario with other divers and instructors, one thing becomes clear: diving with a steel HP100, stainless backplate, and a 7mm wetsuit to 120 feet is not a typical recreational dive. It's a dive profile that edges into technical or advanced territory—even when using standard recreational gear. The equipment choices and environmental factors introduce buoyancy shifts and failure modes that go well beyond entry-level scenarios.

While instructors are trained to meet the needs of their students, it’s important that dive leaders resist the temptation to let their own understanding of dive theory atrophy to match the minimum needed to certify new divers. Problem-solving at depth—especially when equipment fails—demands analytical thinking grounded in physics, physiology, and real-world experience. Agency materials may vary in emphasis, but the individual instructor’s commitment to depth of understanding makes all the difference.

Finally, a diver who has dived to 120 feet, and a diver who has dived in a 7mm wetsuit, may not fully appreciate the risks until the two conditions are combined. Diving to 120 ft in a rash guard in warm Caribbean water is very different from diving to the same depth in a cold lake with a 7mm wetsuit. Likewise, a 30-foot dive in cold water with thick neoprene poses different buoyancy challenges than a deep dive in warm water. These environments compound risks when combined—and divers should be cautious not to assume their comfort with one automatically prepares them for the other.

There’s a well-circulated model describing five stages of subject mastery.

  • Level 0: Oblivious—you don’t know what you don’t know.
  • Level 1: Awareness—you’ve heard about the subject.
  • Level 2: Casual familiarity—maybe you’ve read about it or discussed it informally.
  • Level 3: Studied formally—academic or structured training.
  • Level 4: Taught the subject—repeatedly and interactively, challenged and defended.
The model’s conclusion is striking: true mastery isn’t achieved by studying a subject, but by teaching it. Especially when teaching invites questions, challenges, and varied perspectives from learners.

This has deep implications for diving professionals. A PADI or NAUI instructor who has spent five years teaching only Open Water students may lose touch with the more technical or theoretical aspects they once knew—and worse, may not be aware of that loss. Repetition of material is not reinforcement if it never evolves. As the saying goes: “Ten years of experience isn't worth much if it’s one year of learning followed by nine years of repetition.”

Just as DAN requires recertification for DFA Pro instructors every two years, we might consider whether professional dive educators need periodic technical refreshers—not only to meet agency standards, but to challenge and recalibrate their own understanding. Continuing education is often emphasized for divers. Perhaps it should be equally emphasized for those who lead them.
