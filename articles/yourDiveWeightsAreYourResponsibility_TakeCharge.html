Take Charge of Your Weights: Why Smart Divers Manage Buoyancy Before, During, and After Every Dive

Many instructors and dive guides have the right goals: avoid overweighting new divers, help them develop good buoyancy habits, and keep things safe.

But despite this, we often see instructors and dive guides urging struggling students to "swim down" when they can’t descend at the start of a dive.

The catch? Swimming down head first may work to get underwater—but it introduces a serious risk:

  • It’s harder to equalize inverted.
  • It’s dangerous for your ears and sinuses.
  • Novices may push through discomfort, risking barotrauma.

The False Security of Overweighting

One quick fix you’ll hear:

“Add more weight so you can descend easily.”

Sure, it solves the initial descent issue, but it creates a chain of problems:

  • You need to inflate your BCD more to compensate for the extra weight.
  • An inflated BCD adds drag and makes swimming less efficient.
  • Excessive weight complicates quick responses in an emergency.

And don’t forget: after a long dive, climbing a boat ladder or hiking up a rocky shore with extra lead is exhausting and increases injury risk.

Why End-of-Dive Buoyancy Matters Most

A diver who is neutral at the start of the dive but underweighted at the end faces another risk:

You might find yourself fighting to stay down at your safety stop (15 feet, 500 psi). This is especially common with AL80 tanks, which gain ~4–5 lbs of buoyancy from full to nearly empty.

Good buoyancy management isn’t just about the start of the dive—it’s critical at the end too.

The Right Approach: Diver Responsibility

While it may be hard to change how instructors teach, divers can take control of their own buoyancy:

  • Keep a Dive Log: Track your weight, exposure suit, cylinder type, and conditions. Note if your buoyancy was perfect, too heavy, or too light.
  • Aim for Balance: Weight yourself to be neutral at the safety stop with 500 psi in your tank.
  • Own Your Buoyancy Checks: Regularly review and refine your weighting—don’t rely blindly on others’ advice.

Pro Tip: Safe Descent Technique

Even with proper weighting, sometimes a little encouragement is needed to get under:

  • Stay vertical when descending.
  • Exhale fully but gently.
  • Equalize early and often. Never feel pressured to descend inverted if you’re struggling to clear.

Q&A: Quick Answers to Common Buoyancy Questions

Q: Why is it dangerous to descend head-first when you can’t equalize?

A: Equalizing is harder when inverted because the Eustachian tubes don’t drain or open as easily. This increases the risk of barotrauma, especially for beginners.

Q: Can’t I just overweight myself to make descending easy?

A: Overweighting may help you descend but causes multiple problems: you’ll need a more inflated BCD (more drag), you’ll be harder to rescue in an emergency, and you’ll struggle more with buoyancy control throughout the dive.

Q: How do I know if I’m weighted properly?

A: At the end of a dive with ~500 psi in your tank, you should float at eye level with an empty BCD and a normal breath. When you exhale, you should sink gently.

Q: What’s a good practice for improving my buoyancy?

A: Keep a detailed log of every dive: what gear you used, how much weight, the environment, and how your buoyancy felt. Over time, you’ll spot patterns and fine-tune your setup.

Advanced Tip: Fine-Tuning Your Weight with a Real-World Buoyancy Check

For divers serious about dialing in perfect weighting, there’s one final step that can make a big difference: performing a buoyancy check at the end of a dive with around 500 psi (35 bar) left in the tank.

Ideally, this could be incorporated during your initial Open Water training, using a deep pool or confined water session (12–15 feet depth if available). The concept is simple:

  1. Descend to the bottom and ensure your tank is at ~500 psi.
  2. Slowly remove or add weight in 1-pound increments until you can maintain neutral buoyancy at 15 feet with no air in your BCD, using only your breath to fine-tune position.

Safety Note: Purposely draining your tank to low pressure (500 psi) must be done carefully. Never attempt a low-air buoyancy drill alone. Always dive with a buddy or instructor and stay in a controlled environment. Running a tank low introduces risk, and you must maintain vigilance for out-of-air emergencies.

If your Open Water course doesn’t offer this option, consider doing it after certification as a refresher drill. Rather than purging a tank from the surface, a smarter and safer option is to plan a shallow dive that naturally uses air, then finish the dive by practicing the buoyancy check when you reach ~500 psi.

Fine-tuning your weight this way ensures you’ll be balanced at your safety stop, making every dive safer, easier, and more enjoyable.
