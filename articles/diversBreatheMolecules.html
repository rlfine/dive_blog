We Breathe Molecules of Air, Not PSI

Switch your planning from pressure (PSI/bar) to liters of gas and RMV. Your lungs count molecules, not dial readings.

1) The Core Idea

Pressure is a tool for engineers; molecules are what divers actually consume. Two cylinders at the same gauge pressure can hold very different amounts of usable gas because their internal volumes differ. Planning in liters of gas and RMV (Respiratory Minute Volume) lines up your math with what your body uses.

Quick intuition: at the surface, an RMV of 16 L/min means you breathe about 0.654 mol of gas per minute, i.e., roughly 3.94×1023 molecules per minute. That’s the budget that matters.

2) Ideal Gas Law: the “one ring” that unifies the others

Rather than juggling Dalton’s, Charles’, and Amonton’s laws separately, use the Ideal Gas Law and derive anything you need:

PV = nRT

Where P = absolute pressure (atm), V = volume (L), n = moles, R = 0.082057 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹, T = temperature (K). Use absolute pressure: Pabs = Pgauge + 1 atm. For PSI, 1 atm = 14.7 psi.

3) Molecules in a Cylinder (worked AL80 example)

(a) At 750 psi gauge

P_abs = (750 + 14.7) / 14.7 ≈ 52 atm
n = (P_abs × V) / (R × T) → ~23.6 mol
Molecules ≈ 1.42×10²⁵
Free gas ≈ 52 atm × 11.1 L ≈ 578 L

(b) At 3000 psi gauge

P_abs = (3000 + 14.7) / 14.7 ≈ 206 atm
n ≈ 93.1 mol → Molecules ≈ 5.61×10²⁵
Free gas ≈ 206 atm × 11.1 L ≈ 2277 L

4) Liters & RMV Beat PSI for Planning

RMV is your surface gas use in liters per minute. At the surface with RMV = 16 L/min:

n per min = RMV / (R × T) ≈ 0.654 mol
Molecules/min ≈ 3.94×10²³

At depth, multiply by ATA. Example: 16 L/min × 4 ATA = 64 L/min at 30 m.

6) Why This Framing Helps Older/Mixed-Equipment Divers

  • Different cylinders at the same PSI can have very different liters remaining.
  • RMV × ATA converts liters to minutes, regardless of tank size.
  • Turn pressures become turn liters — more reliable in mixed teams.

7) Takeaways

  • Memorize the ideal gas law. It unifies the classroom laws and powers your field math.
  • Plan in liters + RMV, verify with PSI/bar.
  • Carry your cylinder’s water capacity (V) on a slate; it’s the key to converting PSI → liters → minutes.

Molecule mindset: with RMV 16 L/min, you’re breathing ≈ 3.94×10²³ molecules per minute at the surface.

8) Sidebars: Switching to Bar/Meters + Quick Cheat Sheet

💡 Why Bar + Meters + Liters Makes Math Easier

  • Meters → ATA is trivial: depth (m)/10 + 1. Example: 30 m = 4 ATA.
  • Bar × liters = liters: A 12 L tank at 200 bar ≈ 2400 L. PSI needs conversion first.
  • RMV already in L/min: Multiply RMV × ATA; divide liters by that number for time.
  • Turn pressures become turn liters: 50 bar reserve × tank liters = safety gas in liters.
  • Head-math: 200 bar × 11 L ≈ 2200 L; 230 bar × 13 L ≈ 3000 L.

Bottom line: meters + bar + RMV give instant answers. PSI/feet always need conversions.

🤿 Fun Fact: 200 Molecules in an AL80

Let’s say we somehow trapped only 200 molecules of air in an AL80 tank (about 80% nitrogen = 160 N2 molecules, and 20% oxygen = 40 O2 molecules). Using the ideal gas law at room temperature (298 K) and the AL80 internal volume (~11 L):

n = 200 / (6.022×10²³) mol ≈ 3.32×10⁻²² mol
P = nRT / V = (3.32×10⁻²² × 0.08206 × 298) / 11
P ≈ 7.4×10⁻²² atm
P ≈ 1.1×10⁻²⁰ psi (absolute)

Gauge pressure ≈ –14.7 psi (a near-perfect vacuum). In other words: 200 molecules in an AL80 wouldn’t even nudge the needle off zero!
