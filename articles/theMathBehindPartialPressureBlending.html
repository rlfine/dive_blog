Worked Example: Partial Pressure Blending to Create EAN36

Scenario: A diver performs the requisite equipment pool check, starting with a Steel HP100 tank, filled to 3000 psi of EAN32. During pool check, diver used 1200 psi. Prior to open water dive, diver wants to top off his HP100 tank to 3400 psi of EAN36. In this exercise, we will calculate the amount of pure oxygen to add, and how much air to use for the remainder.

See Cautionary Note ⚠️ below.

  1. Determine target oxygen pressure:
    Final mixture is 36% O2 at 3400 psi:
    3400 × 0.36 = 1224 psi of O₂
  2. Determine existing oxygen pressure:
    Starting mix is 32% O2 at 1800 psi:
    1800 × 0.32 = 576 psi of O₂
  3. Let X = psi of pure O₂ to add, and Y = psi of O₂ from air top-off.
    Total O₂ needed is:
    576 + X + Y = 1224
  4. Top-off air contributes:
    Air adds 21% oxygen, so:
    Y = 0.21 × (3400 − 1800 − X)
    Y = 0.21 × (1600 − X)
  5. Substitute and solve:
    576 + X + 0.21 × (1600 − X) = 1224
    Expand:
    576 + X + 336 − 0.21X = 1224
    Combine like terms:
    912 + 0.79X = 1224
    Solve for X:
    X = (1224 − 912) ÷ 0.79 = 395 psi of pure O₂
  6. Calculate air top-off:
    Total top-off pressure = 3400 − 1800 − 395 = 1205 psi of air
    O₂ from air = 0.21 × 1205 = 253 psi

Result: Add 395 psi of pure O₂, then 1205 psi of air to yield a final tank pressure of 3400 psi with an oxygen fraction of 36% (EAN36).

⚠️ Caution: Real-World Blending Practice

The worked example above is a mathematical exercise to illustrate partial pressure blending calculations. In actual practice, it is not advisable to add pure oxygen to a tank that already contains 1800 psi of EAN32.

For both safety and practical reasons, dive shops following best practices would typically empty the tank to near zero before filling with a new blend like EAN36. Pure O₂ is normally added first to an empty or nearly empty O₂-clean tank, followed by topping off with filtered air to achieve the desired oxygen percentage.

Attempting to add pure O₂ to a partially full tank at high pressure requires specialized equipment such as an O₂ booster pump, should generally be avoided as a standard practice, and if done, should only be done by trained professionals under controlled conditions.
